Article 229 of the Constitution of Pakistan establishes the mechanism by which questions regarding the compatibility of proposed laws with Islamic injunctions can be referred to the Islamic Council for advice. This article is significant because it integrates the principles of Islam into the legislative process, ensuring that laws are in harmony with Islamic teachings. It provides a formal procedure for either the President or the Governor of a Province to seek the Islamic Council's opinion on whether a proposed law aligns with Islamic injunctions. Additionally, it empowers a House or a Provincial Assembly to make such a referral if a certain proportion of its members demand it, thus embedding a democratic element into the process.

Originally, the article required that two-fifths of the total membership of a House or a Provincial Assembly must support the referral to the Islamic Council. However, the Twenty-sixth Amendment, enacted in 2024, reduced this requirement to one-fourth of the total membership. This change effectively lowered the threshold for legislative bodies to seek the Islamic Council's advice, potentially increasing the frequency with which proposed laws are reviewed for their adherence to Islamic principles. The amendment reflects a political decision to make it easier for legislative bodies to engage with the Islamic Council, possibly in response to demands for greater alignment of laws with Islamic values or to streamline the legislative process in this regard. This adjustment could have practical implications by enhancing the role of the Islamic Council in legislative matters and ensuring that a broader range of legislative proposals are scrutinized for their compliance with Islamic injunctions.
