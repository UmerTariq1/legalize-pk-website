Article 212-B of the Constitution of Pakistan was introduced through the Twelfth Amendment on July 28, 1991, during the presidency of Ghulam Ishaq Khan. This article was established to create Special Courts for the speedy trial of heinous offenses that are particularly gruesome, brutal, sensational, or shocking to public morality. The purpose of these courts is to ensure that such serious cases are handled swiftly and efficiently, reflecting the government's intent to maintain public order and morality by addressing crimes that have a significant impact on society.

The article outlines the structure and jurisdiction of these Special Courts. It allows the Federal Government to establish as many Special Courts as necessary and to define their territorial jurisdiction. Judges for these courts are appointed by the Federal Government, in consultation with the Chief Justice of the relevant High Court, and can include individuals who are qualified to be High Court judges. Additionally, the article provides for the establishment of Supreme Appellate Courts to hear appeals from the Special Courts, with a composition that includes a Supreme Court judge and two High Court judges, again appointed in consultation with the Chief Justices concerned.

A significant aspect of Article 212-B is its provision that decisions by these courts must be made within thirty days, emphasizing the need for expediency in handling such cases. Furthermore, it restricts other courts from intervening in the proceedings or decisions of the Special and Supreme Appellate Courts, except as allowed by the law under which these courts are constituted. This provision underscores the autonomy and finality intended for the decisions of these Special Courts, aiming to prevent delays and ensure swift justice in cases of heinous crimes.
