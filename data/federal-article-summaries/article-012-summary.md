Article 12 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is designed to protect individuals from retrospective punishment. This means that a person cannot be punished for an act that was not considered a crime at the time it was committed. Furthermore, if an act was punishable, the punishment cannot be more severe than what was prescribed by the law at the time the act was committed. This provision is crucial as it upholds the principle of legality, ensuring that laws are not applied retroactively to the detriment of individuals. It safeguards citizens from arbitrary state action and ensures fairness in the legal system by preventing the imposition of new laws on past actions.

The article also includes an exception to this rule, stating that the protection against retrospective punishment does not apply to laws that criminalize acts of abrogation or subversion of the Constitution that have occurred since March 23, 1956. This exception reflects the importance placed on maintaining constitutional order and stability in Pakistan. By excluding acts of constitutional subversion from the protection against retrospective punishment, the article underscores the seriousness with which such acts are viewed and the necessity of holding individuals accountable for undermining the constitutional framework, even if such acts were not punishable at the time they were committed.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 12 has not been amended, indicating a consistent commitment to the principles of legal certainty and protection against ex post facto laws. This stability suggests a broad consensus on the importance of these protections in maintaining the rule of law and ensuring that citizens are not subject to arbitrary changes in the legal landscape. The article plays a fundamental role in protecting individual rights and maintaining trust in the legal system by ensuring that laws are predictable and just.
