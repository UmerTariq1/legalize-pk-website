Article 99 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, outlines the procedures for conducting the business of the Federal Government. Its primary purpose is to ensure that orders and instruments executed in the name of the President are properly authenticated and that the Federal Government can efficiently manage its operations. This article is significant because it establishes a framework for the delegation of governmental functions, which is essential for the smooth operation of a large and complex federal system. By allowing the Federal Government to delegate tasks to subordinate officers or authorities, it ensures that government operations can be conducted efficiently and without unnecessary bottlenecks.

The article was originally introduced in the 1973 Constitution and remained unchanged until the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. The amendment made a subtle but important change by explicitly stating that the Federal Government "may make rules" for the allocation and transaction of its business, rather than just regulating it. Additionally, a new clause was added, mandating that the Federal Government "shall also make rules" for these purposes. This change underscored the importance of having clear, formalized procedures for government operations, likely in response to a need for greater transparency and accountability in government processes. The practical effect of these changes was to reinforce the requirement for the Federal Government to establish and follow specific rules, thereby promoting consistency and reliability in the conduct of its business. This amendment was part of a broader effort to enhance governance and decentralize power, reflecting the political climate of the time which favored increased provincial autonomy and clearer delineation of federal responsibilities.
