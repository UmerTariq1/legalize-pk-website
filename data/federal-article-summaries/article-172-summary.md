Article 172 of the Constitution of Pakistan deals with the ownership of property that does not have a rightful owner. Initially, as established in the original 1973 Constitution, the article specified that any ownerless property located within a province would belong to the provincial government, while any such property elsewhere would belong to the federal government. Additionally, it stated that all lands, minerals, and other valuable resources within the continental shelf or under the ocean within Pakistan's territorial waters would be owned by the federal government. This provision was significant as it delineated the ownership rights between provincial and federal governments, ensuring clarity in the management and control of resources, which is crucial for governance and economic planning.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, brought significant changes to Article 172. One key modification was the clarification that the federal government's ownership extended to resources beyond the territorial waters, rather than within them. This change likely aimed to align the constitutional text with international maritime law, which distinguishes between territorial waters and the continental shelf. More importantly, the amendment introduced a new clause that provided for joint and equal ownership of mineral oil and natural gas found within a province or its adjacent territorial waters by both the provincial and federal governments. This change was a major shift towards decentralization, reflecting a political move to grant more autonomy and control over natural resources to the provinces, addressing longstanding demands for greater provincial rights and participation in resource management.

The practical effect of these amendments was to empower provincial governments by giving them a stake in the management and benefits of natural resources, which could lead to more equitable economic development across the country. It also necessitated cooperation between provincial and federal authorities, potentially fostering a more collaborative approach to resource management. This change was part of a broader effort to strengthen provincial autonomy and address regional disparities, which have been a source of political tension in Pakistan.
