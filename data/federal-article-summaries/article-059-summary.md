Article 59 of the Constitution of Pakistan establishes the composition and functioning of the Senate, which is the upper house of the Parliament. Initially, in 1973, the Senate was composed of 63 members, with representation from each Provincial Assembly, the Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA), and the Federal Capital. The Senate plays a crucial role in providing equal representation to all provinces, ensuring that smaller provinces have a voice in the legislative process, which is vital for maintaining the federal structure of Pakistan. The Senate is not subject to dissolution, and its members serve staggered terms to ensure continuity.

Over the years, Article 59 has undergone several amendments, reflecting changes in the political landscape and administrative divisions of Pakistan. The Eighth Amendment in 1985 modified the retirement schedule of Senate members, extending their terms from four to six years and altering the election method for members from the Federal Capital. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 significantly expanded the Senate to 104 members, introducing reserved seats for women, technocrats, and non-Muslims, thereby promoting inclusivity and diversity in representation. This amendment also adjusted the terms and retirement schedules to accommodate the increased membership.

The Twenty-fifth Amendment in 2018 reduced the Senate's size to 96 members by removing representation from FATA following its merger with Khyber Pakhtunkhwa. However, existing FATA members were allowed to complete their terms. The Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025 clarified the expiration of members' terms, standardizing it to the eleventh day of March in the year of term completion. These amendments reflect the evolving political and administrative needs of Pakistan, aiming to ensure fair representation and effective governance through the Senate.
