Article 63 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the disqualifications for membership in the country's Parliament, known as the Majlis-e-Shoora. This article is crucial as it sets the standards for who can and cannot serve as a member of Parliament, thereby ensuring that individuals holding public office meet certain ethical and legal criteria. Initially, the article disqualified individuals on grounds such as mental incapacity, insolvency, loss of Pakistani citizenship, holding a profit-making office under the government, or being disqualified by an Act of Parliament. It also provided a mechanism for resolving disputes about a member's disqualification, involving the Speaker or Chairman and the Chief Election Commissioner.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, significantly expanded the disqualifications listed in Article 63. It introduced new criteria, such as disqualification for those in service of statutory bodies controlled by the government, those convicted of offenses against the ideology or security of Pakistan, and those involved in financial misconduct or default. The amendment also refined the process for addressing disqualification questions, requiring the Speaker or Chairman to refer such matters to the Election Commission within a specified timeframe. These changes were likely motivated by a desire to enhance the integrity and accountability of Parliament members, reflecting broader political and social concerns about corruption and governance in Pakistan. The amendment's comprehensive approach to disqualification underscores the importance of maintaining a Parliament composed of individuals who adhere to high ethical and legal standards.
