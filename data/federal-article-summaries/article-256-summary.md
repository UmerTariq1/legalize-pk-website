Article 256 of the Constitution of Pakistan, established in the original 1973 document, addresses the prohibition of private armies. This article explicitly forbids the formation of any private organization that could operate as a military entity, declaring such organizations illegal. The purpose of this article is to ensure that the monopoly on military force remains with the state, thereby maintaining the government's control over armed forces and preventing the rise of private militias that could challenge state authority or disrupt public order.

The inclusion of this article in the 1973 Constitution reflects a commitment to maintaining national security and public safety by preventing the fragmentation of military power. It underscores the importance of a unified and state-controlled military apparatus, which is crucial for the stability and sovereignty of Pakistan. By prohibiting private armies, the article aims to avert the risks associated with armed groups that could potentially engage in activities contrary to national interests or lead to internal conflict.

Since its inception, Article 256 has remained unchanged, indicating a consistent stance by successive governments on the issue of private military organizations. The lack of amendments suggests a broad consensus on the necessity of this provision to uphold the rule of law and ensure that military power is not decentralized or used for unauthorized purposes. This stability in the legal framework helps reinforce the state's authority and its capacity to maintain peace and order within the country.
