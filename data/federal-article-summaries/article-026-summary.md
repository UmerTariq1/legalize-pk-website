Article 26 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is a fundamental rights provision that addresses the issue of non-discrimination in access to public places. It ensures that all citizens have equal access to places of public entertainment or resort, provided these are not exclusively for religious purposes. The article explicitly prohibits discrimination based on race, religion, caste, sex, residence, or place of birth. This provision is crucial as it upholds the principle of equality, a cornerstone of democratic societies, by ensuring that all individuals, regardless of their background, have the same rights to access public spaces. This is particularly significant in a diverse country like Pakistan, where multiple ethnicities, religions, and cultures coexist.

The article also includes a clause that allows the State to make special provisions for women and children. This exception acknowledges the need for protective measures for these groups, who may require additional support to ensure their full and equal participation in public life. By allowing for such provisions, the article balances the principle of non-discrimination with the necessity of affirmative action to promote equality in practice.

Since its inclusion in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 26 has not been amended. This stability suggests a continued commitment to the principles of equality and non-discrimination in public access. The article plays a vital role in promoting social cohesion and inclusivity, reinforcing the idea that public spaces should be accessible to all citizens, thereby fostering a more equitable society.
