Article 157 of the Constitution of Pakistan deals with the regulation and management of electricity, a crucial resource for the country's development and daily functioning. Initially established in the 1973 Constitution, this article outlines the roles and responsibilities of both the Federal and Provincial Governments concerning electricity generation and distribution. The Federal Government is empowered to construct hydro-electric or thermal power installations and lay inter-provincial transmission lines. Meanwhile, Provincial Governments have the authority to manage electricity supply within their territories, levy taxes on electricity consumption, construct power infrastructure, and set tariffs for electricity distribution. This division of responsibilities is significant as it balances power between the central and provincial authorities, ensuring that both have a role in managing this vital sector.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, introduced notable changes to Article 157. One key amendment required the Federal Government to consult with the concerned Provincial Government before deciding to construct hydro-electric power stations within a province. This change was likely motivated by the need to respect provincial autonomy and ensure that local interests and environmental considerations are taken into account in federal decisions. Additionally, the amendment introduced a mechanism for resolving disputes between the Federal and Provincial Governments through the Council of Common Interests. This provision is important as it provides a formal avenue for addressing disagreements, thereby promoting cooperative federalism and reducing potential conflicts over electricity management.

Overall, Article 157 and its amendments reflect the evolving dynamics of federal-provincial relations in Pakistan, particularly in the context of resource management. By delineating clear roles and introducing consultative and dispute resolution mechanisms, the article aims to foster collaboration and ensure efficient and equitable electricity distribution across the country. This is crucial for Pakistan's socio-economic development, as electricity is a fundamental driver of industrial growth, technological advancement, and improved quality of life for its citizens.
