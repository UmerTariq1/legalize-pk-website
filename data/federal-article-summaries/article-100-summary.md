Article 100 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the role and appointment of the Attorney-General for Pakistan. Initially established in the 1973 Constitution, this article provides that the President appoints the Attorney-General, who must be qualified to serve as a judge of the Supreme Court. The Attorney-General serves at the President's pleasure, meaning they can be dismissed at any time. Their primary responsibilities include advising the Federal Government on legal matters and representing the government in all courts and tribunals across Pakistan. The Attorney-General also has the right to resign by submitting a written notice to the President. This role is crucial as it ensures that the government receives expert legal advice and representation, maintaining the rule of law and the proper functioning of the judiciary.

The article has undergone two significant amendments. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 introduced a restriction that the Attorney-General cannot engage in private practice while holding office. This change was likely made to prevent conflicts of interest and ensure the Attorney-General's full commitment to their governmental duties. The Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025 further altered the qualifications for the position, requiring that the Attorney-General be eligible to be appointed as a judge of the Federal Constitutional Court instead of the Supreme Court. This shift reflects a possible restructuring or emphasis on constitutional matters within the legal framework of Pakistan, indicating a focus on ensuring that the Attorney-General has expertise in constitutional law. These amendments highlight the evolving nature of the role to adapt to the changing legal and political landscape of Pakistan.
