Article 21 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is designed to protect individuals from being compelled to financially support religions other than their own through taxation. This article ensures that no person is obligated to pay a special tax if the funds collected are intended for the promotion or upkeep of a religion that is not theirs. The purpose of this provision is to uphold the principle of religious freedom and prevent the state from imposing financial burdens on individuals for religious activities they do not subscribe to. It reflects the commitment to maintaining a secular approach in matters of taxation, ensuring that citizens are not forced to contribute to religious causes that do not align with their personal beliefs.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 21 has remained unchanged, indicating a consistent recognition of its importance in safeguarding religious freedom and financial autonomy. The absence of amendments suggests that the original framers of the Constitution, under the leadership of Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, established a clear and enduring principle that has continued to be relevant and uncontested. This stability underscores the article's role in promoting a pluralistic society where individuals are free to practice their religion without state-imposed financial obligations towards other religions. By preventing the imposition of such taxes, the article helps maintain harmony and respect among diverse religious communities in Pakistan.
