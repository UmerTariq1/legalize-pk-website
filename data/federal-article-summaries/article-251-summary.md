Article 251 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes Urdu as the national language of Pakistan. The article mandates that arrangements should be made for Urdu to be used for official and other purposes within fifteen years from the commencement of the Constitution. This reflects an effort to promote a unified national identity through a common language, which is significant in a country with diverse linguistic groups. The article also allows for the continued use of English for official purposes until Urdu fully replaces it, acknowledging the practical challenges of such a transition and the historical context of English as a colonial legacy and administrative language.

Furthermore, Article 251 provides flexibility to the provinces by allowing them to legislate for the teaching, promotion, and use of provincial languages alongside Urdu. This clause recognizes the linguistic diversity of Pakistan and the importance of regional languages in cultural and educational contexts. By permitting provincial assemblies to promote their languages, the article balances national unity with regional identity, ensuring that local languages and cultures are preserved and promoted.

Since its inception, Article 251 has not been amended, indicating a consistent constitutional stance on the language policy. The article's provisions have practical implications for education, administration, and governance, as they require significant efforts in language planning and policy implementation. The gradual transition from English to Urdu in official domains has been a complex process, influenced by political, social, and economic factors, and remains a topic of ongoing discussion in Pakistan.
