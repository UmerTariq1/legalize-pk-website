Article 97 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 outlines the extent of the executive authority of the Federation. This article establishes the scope of the federal government's power, specifying that it can exercise executive authority over matters where the Parliament has the power to legislate. This includes the exercise of rights and jurisdiction in areas outside of Pakistan. The article is crucial as it delineates the boundary between federal and provincial powers, ensuring a clear division of responsibilities and preventing overreach by the federal government into areas that are within the jurisdiction of provincial governments.

The article also includes a proviso that restricts the federal authority from extending into matters within a province's legislative competence unless explicitly allowed by the Constitution or a law made by Parliament. This provision is significant as it safeguards provincial autonomy, ensuring that provinces retain control over certain legislative areas. The balance of power between the federal and provincial governments is a fundamental aspect of Pakistan's federal structure, and Article 97 plays a key role in maintaining this balance.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 97 has not been amended, indicating that the original framework for the division of executive authority between the Federation and the provinces has remained intact. This stability suggests that the original provisions have been effective in managing the distribution of power and responsibilities within Pakistan's federal system. The article's enduring nature underscores its importance in the constitutional framework, providing a consistent basis for federal-provincial relations over the decades.
