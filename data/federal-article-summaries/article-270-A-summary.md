Article 270-A of the Constitution of Pakistan was introduced through the Eighth Amendment in 1985 during the regime of General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq. This article served to validate and legitimize all actions, orders, and laws enacted under the martial law period that began on July 5, 1977. It essentially provided constitutional cover to the various presidential orders, ordinances, and martial law regulations issued during this period, ensuring that they could not be challenged in any court. The article was a crucial part of Zia's efforts to stabilize his regime by embedding his legal and political decisions within the constitutional framework, thereby preventing any legal repercussions for actions taken during his rule.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, under President Asif Ali Zardari, made significant changes to Article 270-A. The amendment removed the specific mention of the referendum that had legitimized Zia-ul-Haq's presidency, reflecting a shift away from endorsing the actions of the military regime. Additionally, it simplified the process for amending the laws validated by Article 270-A, allowing the appropriate legislature to amend these laws in the manner provided for their amendment, rather than requiring a constitutional amendment. This change signified a move towards greater legislative flexibility and a step away from the rigid structures imposed by the martial law period.

The evolution of Article 270-A highlights the tension between military and civilian rule in Pakistan's history. Initially, it was a tool for military rulers to cement their authority and protect their actions from judicial scrutiny. However, the changes introduced by the Eighteenth Amendment reflect a democratic shift, aiming to restore parliamentary sovereignty and reduce the lingering influence of past military regimes on the country's legal and constitutional framework. This transition underscores the ongoing struggle in Pakistan to balance power between military and civilian institutions.
