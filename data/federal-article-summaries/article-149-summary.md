Article 149 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is concerned with the relationship between the federal government and the provincial governments, specifically in terms of the federal government's authority to issue directions to the provinces. This article is significant because it outlines the circumstances under which the federal government can intervene in provincial matters, ensuring that provincial actions do not hinder federal operations. It also allows the federal government to direct provinces in executing federal laws, maintaining communication infrastructure of national importance, and addressing threats to peace, tranquility, or economic stability.

The evolution of Article 149 reflects a shift in the balance of power between the federal and provincial governments. Originally, the article allowed the federal government to direct provinces on matters in the Concurrent Legislative List, which included subjects on which both federal and provincial governments could legislate. However, the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 removed this provision, thereby reducing the federal government's direct influence over provincial matters listed in the Concurrent Legislative List. This amendment was part of a broader effort to decentralize power and grant more autonomy to the provinces, reflecting a political move towards strengthening provincial governance and addressing longstanding demands for greater provincial rights. The practical effect of this change was to enhance provincial autonomy and limit federal intervention, thereby promoting a more balanced federal structure in Pakistan.
