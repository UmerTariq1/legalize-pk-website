Article 115 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, outlines the procedural requirements for introducing financial legislation within a provincial assembly. Its primary purpose is to ensure that any financial measures, specifically those that would impact the Provincial Consolidated Fund or the Public Account of the Province, are introduced with the consent of the provincial government. This article is crucial because it establishes a system of checks and balances, ensuring that financial decisions are made with the provincial government's approval, thereby preventing unilateral financial commitments that could affect the province's fiscal stability.

The article defines what constitutes a Money Bill, which includes legislation related to taxation, borrowing, and financial obligations of the provincial government. It also specifies that certain financial matters, such as fines or local taxes, do not classify a bill as a Money Bill. The Speaker of the Provincial Assembly holds the authority to determine whether a bill qualifies as a Money Bill, and this decision is final. Additionally, any Money Bill must be certified by the Speaker before being presented to the Governor for assent, ensuring that the legislative process is adhered to and that there is no ambiguity regarding the bill's financial implications.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 115 has not been amended, indicating its foundational role in the financial governance of provinces in Pakistan. By requiring provincial government consent for financial legislation, the article safeguards provincial autonomy and ensures that financial measures are aligned with the province's economic policies and priorities. This framework is vital for maintaining fiscal discipline and accountability within provincial governments, ultimately contributing to the broader constitutional structure of Pakistan.
