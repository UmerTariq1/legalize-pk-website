Article 76 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the continuity of legislative processes in the event of prorogation or dissolution of the National Assembly. Its primary purpose is to ensure that legislative efforts are not entirely disrupted by the procedural end of a parliamentary session or the dissolution of the National Assembly. This article is crucial because it maintains legislative momentum and prevents the wastage of parliamentary time and resources on bills that are in progress.

The article establishes three key rules: First, a bill that is pending in either the National Assembly or the Senate does not lapse simply because the House is prorogued, meaning temporarily suspended. This ensures that the legislative process can pick up where it left off once the House reconvenes. Second, if a bill is pending in the Senate but has not yet been passed by the National Assembly, it does not lapse even if the National Assembly is dissolved. This provision allows the Senate to continue its work on the bill, preserving the legislative effort. Lastly, a bill that is either pending in the National Assembly or has been passed by the National Assembly but is pending in the Senate will lapse if the National Assembly is dissolved. This rule reflects the principle that the National Assembly, being the directly elected house, has a primary role in the legislative process, and its dissolution resets the legislative agenda.

The article has remained unchanged since its inception in 1973, indicating a stable consensus on the importance of these procedural rules. By preventing the automatic lapse of bills due to prorogation or dissolution, Article 76 plays a vital role in ensuring legislative continuity and efficiency in Pakistan's parliamentary system.
