Article 196 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the appointment of an Acting Chief Justice for a High Court. This provision is crucial for maintaining the continuity and stability of the judiciary at the provincial level. It ensures that the functions of the Chief Justice are not disrupted due to vacancy, absence, or incapacity. The article empowers the President to appoint an acting Chief Justice, thereby providing a mechanism to uphold the rule of law and the administration of justice without interruption.

Initially, the article mandated that the President appoint the most senior judge of the High Court as the Acting Chief Justice. However, this was amended by the Fifth Amendment in 1976, which altered the criteria for such appointments. The amendment allowed the President to appoint any judge of the High Court, provided that the judge had not previously served as Chief Justice except in an acting capacity under this article. This change was likely motivated by a desire to introduce flexibility in appointments and to prevent the automatic elevation of the most senior judge, which could have been seen as limiting the President's discretion or potentially perpetuating a status quo that might not always serve the best interests of the judiciary or the public.

The practical effect of this amendment was to broaden the pool of eligible candidates for the role of Acting Chief Justice, allowing for a more dynamic and potentially merit-based selection process. This change could have been politically motivated to allow the executive branch more influence over judicial appointments, reflecting the broader context of political maneuvering and control over key state institutions during that period in Pakistan's history.
