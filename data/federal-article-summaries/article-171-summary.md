Article 171 of the Constitution of Pakistan pertains to the reports of the Auditor-General, a crucial component in ensuring financial transparency and accountability within the government. The Auditor-General is responsible for auditing the accounts of the Federation and the Provinces, and the findings are reported to the respective heads of state. Initially, as per the 1973 Constitution, these reports were to be submitted to the President for the Federation and to the Governor for the Provinces. The President was then required to present these reports to the National Assembly, while the Governor was to present them to the Provincial Assembly. This process was designed to ensure that elected representatives were informed about the financial management and expenditures of the government, thereby enabling them to perform their oversight functions effectively.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, brought a significant change to this article. It expanded the requirement for the submission of the Auditor-General's reports at the federal level. Instead of being laid solely before the National Assembly, the reports were now to be presented before both Houses of the Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament), which includes the Senate in addition to the National Assembly. This amendment was part of a broader effort to enhance democratic processes and transparency in Pakistan's governance. By involving both Houses of Parliament, the amendment aimed to ensure a more comprehensive review and discussion of the financial audits, thereby strengthening the accountability mechanisms within the federal government. This change reflects a political commitment to increased transparency and a more inclusive legislative process, allowing for a wider range of parliamentary scrutiny and debate on financial matters.
