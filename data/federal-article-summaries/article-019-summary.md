Article 19 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is dedicated to the fundamental right of freedom of speech and expression. It establishes that every citizen has the right to express their thoughts freely and that the press should operate without undue restrictions. However, this freedom is not absolute and can be curtailed by the state under specific circumstances. The article outlines several grounds on which restrictions can be imposed, including the glory of Islam, national security, public order, decency, morality, and maintaining friendly relations with other states. These limitations are intended to balance individual freedoms with the broader interests of society and the state, reflecting the complex interplay between personal liberties and national priorities in Pakistan's constitutional framework.

The Fourth Amendment to the Constitution, enacted in 1975, introduced a subtle yet significant change to Article 19. The amendment added the phrase "commission of" alongside "incitement to an offence," thereby expanding the scope of restrictions that could be applied to freedom of speech. This change meant that not only could speech that incited an offence be restricted, but also speech that involved the commission of an offence. This amendment likely aimed to address concerns about speech that could directly lead to criminal activities, thereby enhancing the state's ability to maintain law and order. The political context of the time, marked by concerns over internal security and political stability, may have influenced this amendment, reflecting the government's intent to tighten control over potentially destabilizing expressions.
