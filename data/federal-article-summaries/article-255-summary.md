Article 255 of the Constitution of Pakistan pertains to the oath of office, which is a formal promise or declaration made by individuals before they assume certain official positions. This article is crucial as it ensures that individuals entering public office acknowledge their responsibilities and commit to upholding the Constitution and the laws of Pakistan. The oath serves as a symbolic and legal affirmation of loyalty and duty, reinforcing the integrity and accountability of public officials. It is a foundational element in maintaining the rule of law and the proper functioning of governmental institutions.

Originally, Article 255 specified that the oath must be made in a language understood by the person taking it, ensuring clarity and comprehension. It also provided flexibility in the administration of the oath, allowing it to be taken before an alternative person if it was impractical to do so before the originally specified individual. This flexibility was crucial for maintaining the continuity of governance, especially in unforeseen circumstances where the designated official might be unavailable.

The Twenty-sixth Amendment in 2024 introduced a significant change to this article. It specified that if an oath could not be made before the designated person, the alternative person must be nominated by the Chief Justice of a High Court for provincial matters, and by the Chief Justice of Pakistan for all other cases. This amendment aimed to standardize the process and ensure that the nomination of an alternative official for administering oaths is handled by the highest judicial authorities, thereby enhancing the credibility and impartiality of the process. This change reflects an effort to reinforce the judiciary's role in upholding constitutional procedures and ensuring that the transition of officeholders is conducted with due diligence and respect for the rule of law.
