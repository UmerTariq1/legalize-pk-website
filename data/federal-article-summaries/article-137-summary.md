Article 137 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 outlines the extent of the executive authority of a province. This article is crucial as it delineates the powers and responsibilities of provincial governments, ensuring a clear division of authority between the federal and provincial levels. According to this article, a province's executive authority covers all matters on which its Provincial Assembly can legislate. This provision is significant because it empowers provinces to manage their own affairs in areas where they have legislative competence, promoting a degree of autonomy and decentralization within the federal structure of Pakistan.

However, the article also establishes a hierarchy of authority in cases where both the federal Parliament and a Provincial Assembly have the power to legislate on the same matter. In such instances, the province's executive authority is constrained by the executive authority that the Constitution or federal law explicitly grants to the federal government. This ensures that while provinces have autonomy, there is a mechanism to maintain national coherence and prevent conflicts between provincial and federal laws. This balance is essential for maintaining the unity of the state while respecting the diversity and autonomy of its provinces.

Since its inception in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 137 has not been amended. This stability suggests that the original framers' intent to balance provincial autonomy with federal oversight has remained relevant and effective in managing the division of powers within Pakistan's federal system. The article's enduring nature underscores its foundational role in defining the relationship between provincial and federal authorities, which is a cornerstone of Pakistan's governance.
