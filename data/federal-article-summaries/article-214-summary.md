Article 214 of the Constitution of Pakistan, originally established in 1973, outlines the requirement for certain officials to take an oath of office before assuming their duties. Initially, this article specifically addressed the oath-taking process for a Commissioner, who was required to make an oath before the Chief Justice of Pakistan. This provision is significant as it underscores the importance of accountability and adherence to constitutional duties by high-ranking officials, ensuring they commit to uphold the laws and principles of the state before commencing their official responsibilities.

The article underwent a notable change with the Twentieth Amendment in 2012. This amendment expanded the scope of the oath requirement to include not only Commissioners but also members of the Election Commission. Now, while Commissioners continue to take their oath before the Chief Justice, members of the Election Commission are required to take their oath before the Commissioner. This change reflects a broader commitment to ensuring that all individuals involved in the electoral process are bound by a formal pledge to perform their duties with integrity and impartiality. The amendment likely aimed to strengthen the credibility and independence of the Election Commission, an institution critical to the democratic process in Pakistan, especially in light of political dynamics and the need for transparent electoral practices.
