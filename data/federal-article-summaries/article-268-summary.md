Article 268 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the continuity and adaptation of existing laws following the adoption of the new Constitution. Its primary purpose is to ensure legal stability and continuity by allowing pre-existing laws to remain in effect, subject to necessary modifications, until they are formally altered or repealed by the appropriate legislative body. This provision is crucial as it prevents a legal vacuum that could arise from the immediate invalidation of all previous laws upon the enactment of the new Constitution. It also empowers the President to make necessary adaptations to align existing laws with the new constitutional framework, ensuring a smooth transition.

The article has undergone significant changes through constitutional amendments. The Seventeenth Amendment in 2003, under President Pervez Musharraf, introduced a clause that allowed certain laws listed in the Sixth Schedule to be omitted after six years, reflecting a move towards reducing presidential control over specific legislative matters. This change was likely influenced by the political context of Musharraf's regime, which sought to balance power between the executive and legislative branches. Later, the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, under President Asif Ali Zardari, further altered Article 268 by removing the requirement for presidential sanction to amend laws in the Sixth Schedule. This amendment was part of a broader effort to decentralize power and enhance parliamentary sovereignty, reflecting a shift towards strengthening democratic institutions and reducing the concentration of power in the executive branch.

These amendments have had practical effects on the governance structure in Pakistan. By removing certain presidential controls, they have empowered the legislature and provincial governments, promoting a more balanced distribution of power. This evolution reflects Pakistan's ongoing journey towards a more democratic and decentralized governance model, aiming to enhance the responsiveness and accountability of its political institutions.
