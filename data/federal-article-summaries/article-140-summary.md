Article 140 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the role of the Advocate-General for each province. This position is crucial as it serves as the chief legal advisor to the provincial government. The Advocate-General is appointed by the Governor of the province and must be qualified to be a judge of the High Court. The primary responsibilities include advising the provincial government on legal matters and performing other legal duties as assigned. This role is significant in ensuring that the provincial government's actions are legally sound and that it receives expert legal guidance.

The article was originally part of the 1973 Constitution and remained unchanged until the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. This amendment introduced a critical change: it prohibited the Advocate-General from engaging in private legal practice while holding office. This change was likely made to prevent conflicts of interest and ensure that the Advocate-General's focus remains solely on serving the provincial government. By restricting private practice, the amendment aimed to enhance the integrity and impartiality of the office, ensuring that the Advocate-General's advice and actions are not influenced by external legal engagements. This change reflects a broader effort to strengthen the accountability and professionalism of public office holders in Pakistan.
