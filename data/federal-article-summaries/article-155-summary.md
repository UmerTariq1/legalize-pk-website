Article 155 of the Constitution of Pakistan addresses the issue of water supply interference, providing a mechanism for resolving disputes when the water interests of a province, the Federal Capital, or their inhabitants are adversely affected. This article is crucial because water is a vital resource, and its distribution can significantly impact agriculture, industry, and daily life. The article empowers the Federal or Provincial Government to lodge complaints with a Council if they believe their water interests are being compromised by legislative or executive actions or by the inaction of relevant authorities. The Council, after reviewing the complaint, can either make a decision or request the President to form a commission of experts to investigate the matter further. The decisions made by the Council are binding, and the article explicitly prevents court proceedings on matters that fall under its purview, ensuring that water disputes are resolved within this framework.

The article has undergone two significant amendments. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 expanded the scope of the article by including water from reservoirs, not just natural sources, reflecting the growing importance of reservoirs in water management. This change likely aimed to address the complexities of modern water storage and distribution systems. The Twenty-fifth Amendment in 2018 removed references to the Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA), following their merger with Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province. This amendment was part of a broader constitutional change to integrate FATA into the provincial framework, thereby streamlining administrative processes and ensuring uniform governance across the region. These amendments reflect the evolving political and administrative landscape of Pakistan and the need to adapt constitutional provisions to current realities.
