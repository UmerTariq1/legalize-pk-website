Article 37 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is focused on promoting social justice and eradicating social evils. It outlines the responsibilities of the State to improve the educational and economic interests of disadvantaged classes and areas, eliminate illiteracy, and ensure access to education and justice. The article also emphasizes the importance of humane working conditions, the prevention of social vices such as prostitution and gambling, and the regulation of alcohol consumption. Additionally, it calls for the decentralization of government administration to better serve the public's needs. This article is significant as it lays down the framework for the State's role in fostering an equitable society and addressing social inequalities, which are crucial for the country's development and cohesion.

The article underwent a notable change with the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. Originally, it included a broader set of directives for the State, such as securing the well-being of people by preventing wealth concentration, providing social security, and ensuring basic necessities for those unable to earn a livelihood. These provisions were removed in the amendment, which streamlined the article to focus more on the decentralization of government administration. The practical effect of this change was a shift in emphasis from broad socio-economic goals to more specific administrative reforms, reflecting a political decision to prioritize governance efficiency over direct socio-economic interventions. This amendment likely aimed to address administrative bottlenecks and improve government responsiveness, which can indirectly contribute to social justice by making state services more accessible and effective.
