Article 11 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 is a fundamental provision that addresses the prohibition of slavery, forced labor, and child labor, reflecting the country's commitment to human rights and dignity. This article is crucial as it establishes the legal framework to ensure that no individual is subjected to slavery or forced labor, which are recognized as severe violations of human rights. It explicitly forbids the existence and introduction of slavery in any form within Pakistan, thereby aligning with international human rights standards.

The article also prohibits all forms of forced labor and human trafficking, which are critical issues in safeguarding the freedom and dignity of individuals. By banning these practices, the article aims to protect vulnerable populations from exploitation and abuse. Furthermore, it sets a clear age limit by prohibiting the employment of children under fourteen years in factories, mines, or any hazardous work, thereby promoting child welfare and education.

While the article firmly prohibits these practices, it makes an exception for compulsory service, which is allowed under specific conditions, such as for individuals serving a legal punishment or for public purposes. However, it ensures that such compulsory service is not cruel or degrading, maintaining respect for human dignity. Since its inception in 1973, Article 11 has remained unchanged, underscoring its foundational role in the constitutional and legal framework of Pakistan in protecting human rights and promoting social justice.
