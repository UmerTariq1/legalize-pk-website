Article 56 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, originally established the President's authority to address either the National Assembly or the Senate, or both Houses when assembled together. This provision underscores the ceremonial and communicative role of the President within the parliamentary system, allowing the President to directly engage with the legislative branch. The ability to require the attendance of members for such an address highlights the importance of the President's messages, which can encompass matters of national significance or government policy.

The Eighth Amendment, enacted in 1985 during the regime of General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq, expanded this article significantly. It introduced a mandatory requirement for the President to address both Houses of Parliament at the commencement of the first session after each general election and at the beginning of each parliamentary year. This change institutionalized the President's address as a regular feature of parliamentary proceedings, ensuring that the President provides an overview of the government's agenda and the reasons for summoning the Parliament. This amendment was part of broader constitutional changes during Zia-ul-Haq's rule, which aimed to strengthen the executive's influence over the legislative process and ensure that the President's role in guiding parliamentary priorities was formally recognized. The practical effect of this change was to create a structured opportunity for the President to set the legislative tone and priorities for the upcoming sessions, thereby influencing the legislative agenda and maintaining a channel of communication between the executive and legislative branches.
