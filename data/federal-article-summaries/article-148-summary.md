Article 148 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the obligations of both the provinces and the federal government in maintaining a cohesive and functional relationship within the country's federal structure. This article is crucial as it delineates the responsibilities and powers between the provincial and federal levels of government, ensuring that federal laws are implemented across all provinces while also safeguarding provincial interests. It emphasizes the need for provincial executive authorities to comply with federal laws applicable within their territories, thereby maintaining a unified legal framework across the nation.

Furthermore, Article 148 mandates that while the federal government exercises its authority in any province, it must consider the interests of that province. This provision ensures that federal actions are not only in line with national interests but also sensitive to the unique needs and circumstances of each province. Additionally, the article places a duty on the federal government to protect provinces from external threats and internal unrest, ensuring stability and constitutional governance throughout the country. This protective clause underscores the federal government's role in maintaining peace and order, which is vital for the country's integrity and the well-being of its citizens.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 148 has not been amended, indicating its foundational role in the constitutional framework of Pakistan. Its unaltered state suggests a consensus on the importance of balancing federal authority with provincial autonomy, a balance that is essential for the functioning of a federal state like Pakistan. By clearly defining these obligations, Article 148 helps prevent conflicts between different levels of government and promotes cooperative federalism, which is key to the country's stability and development.
