Article 147 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the power of provincial governments to delegate their functions to the federal government. This provision allows a province, with the consent of the federal government, to transfer responsibilities related to matters within its executive authority to the federal level. The purpose of this article is to facilitate cooperation and flexibility between provincial and federal governments, ensuring that certain functions can be managed at the federal level when necessary. This is particularly important in a federal structure like Pakistan's, where the division of powers between different levels of government must be balanced to maintain effective governance.

The article was originally part of the 1973 Constitution and remained unchanged until the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. The amendment introduced a significant change by adding a requirement for provincial assemblies to ratify any functions entrusted to the federal government within sixty days. This change aimed to enhance provincial autonomy and ensure that such decisions have the backing of the elected representatives of the province. The amendment reflects a broader political shift towards decentralization and empowerment of provincial governments, which was a key theme of the Eighteenth Amendment. By requiring ratification, the amendment ensures greater accountability and transparency in the delegation of provincial functions to the federal government, thereby strengthening democratic processes at the provincial level.
