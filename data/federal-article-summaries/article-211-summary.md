Article 211 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 establishes a legal principle known as the "bar of jurisdiction." This article specifically states that the proceedings of the Supreme Judicial Council, its report to the President, and the removal of a judge under Article 209 cannot be challenged in any court. The purpose of this provision is to ensure the independence and integrity of the judicial accountability process by preventing external interference or judicial review of the Council's decisions. This is crucial in maintaining the separation of powers, as it allows the judiciary to self-regulate and address issues of misconduct or incapacity within its ranks without the influence of other branches of government.

Since its inception in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 211 has remained unchanged. This stability suggests a continued consensus on the importance of protecting the judicial accountability process from being questioned or overturned by other courts. By insulating the decisions of the Supreme Judicial Council from judicial review, the article aims to uphold the authority and finality of the Council's decisions, thereby reinforcing the judiciary's autonomy. This provision is significant for maintaining public confidence in the judiciary's ability to self-govern and ensure that judges adhere to the highest standards of conduct without external pressures.
