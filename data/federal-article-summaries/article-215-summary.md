Article 215 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 originally established the term of office for the Commissioner, who was to serve for three years, with the possibility of a one-year extension by the National Assembly. The article also outlined the process for removal and resignation, aligning the removal process with that of a judge, ensuring a degree of protection and independence for the Commissioner.

Over time, this article has undergone several amendments, reflecting changes in the political and administrative landscape of Pakistan. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 extended the term of office from three to five years, indicating a shift towards longer tenures for stability and continuity in office. The Twentieth Amendment in 2012 expanded the scope of the article to include not just the Commissioner but also other members, ensuring that the same rules applied to all members of the commission. This change was likely aimed at creating a uniform structure and process for all members, enhancing the governance framework.

The Twenty-second Amendment in 2016 introduced a staggered retirement system for members, with half retiring every two and a half years. This was designed to ensure continuity and prevent a complete turnover at any one time, which could disrupt the functioning of the commission. Additionally, it mandated that vacancies be filled within 45 days, ensuring that the commission remains fully operational. The Twenty-sixth Amendment in 2024 further refined the article by allowing the Commissioner and members to continue in office until successors are appointed, preventing any potential gaps in leadership. These amendments collectively reflect an evolving understanding of the need for stability, continuity, and efficiency in the functioning of the commission, which plays a crucial role in the governance and administrative framework of Pakistan.
