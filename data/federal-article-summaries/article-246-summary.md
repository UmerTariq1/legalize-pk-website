Article 246 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 originally defined the "Tribal Areas" within the country, categorizing them into Provincially Administered Tribal Areas (PATA) and Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA). These areas were historically significant due to their unique administrative status and autonomy, which stemmed from colonial times. The article outlined the specific districts and agencies that fell under these categories, reflecting the distinct governance and legal frameworks applicable to these regions. This was crucial in the constitutional structure as it recognized the unique cultural and administrative needs of these areas, which were not fully integrated into the provincial governance systems.

Over the years, Article 246 underwent several amendments, reflecting changing political and administrative priorities. The Sixth Amendment in 1976 made minor adjustments, such as including the Orakzai Agency in FATA and refining the boundaries of PATA. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 further expanded the definition of Tribal Areas by including regions adjoining Lakki Marwat and Tank districts. The Nineteenth Amendment in 2011 refined these definitions, ensuring more precise administrative delineations. These changes were part of broader efforts to address governance issues and integrate these areas more effectively into the national framework.

The most significant change came with the Twenty-fifth Amendment in 2018, which marked a historic shift by merging FATA and parts of PATA into the provinces of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and Balochistan. This merger aimed to bring these areas into the mainstream legal and administrative framework of Pakistan, providing their residents with the same rights and responsibilities as other citizens. This amendment was a response to long-standing demands for integration and development, aiming to improve governance, security, and socio-economic conditions in these historically marginalized regions.
