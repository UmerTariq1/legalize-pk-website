Article 3 of the Constitution of Pakistan, established in 1973, is focused on the elimination of exploitation within the country. This article mandates the state to work towards eradicating all forms of exploitation and to progressively realize the principle of "from each according to his ability to each according to his work." This principle is rooted in the idea of social justice, aiming to ensure that individuals contribute to society based on their abilities and receive rewards based on their work. It reflects a commitment to creating a fair and equitable society where resources and opportunities are distributed justly, reducing disparities and preventing the misuse of power or resources by any group or individual.

The inclusion of this article in the original 1973 Constitution under the leadership of Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto highlights the emphasis on social justice and economic equity during that period. Bhutto's government was known for its socialist-leaning policies, which aimed to address the socio-economic inequalities prevalent in Pakistan. By embedding this principle in the Constitution, the framers sought to provide a constitutional basis for policies aimed at reducing exploitation and promoting economic justice.

Since its inception, Article 3 has not been amended, indicating a continued commitment to its foundational principles. Its presence in the Constitution serves as a guiding framework for legislative and policy measures aimed at addressing exploitation and promoting equitable economic development. While the practical implementation of this article's ideals may vary, its constitutional mandate underscores the importance of striving towards a society where exploitation is minimized, and fairness in economic participation is prioritized.
