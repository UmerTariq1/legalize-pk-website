Article 273 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 initially established the framework for the first Provincial Assemblies following the adoption of the Constitution. Its primary purpose was to ensure a smooth transition from the Interim Constitution to the new constitutional framework by allowing the existing provincial assemblies to continue functioning until August 14, 1977. This continuity was crucial for maintaining legislative stability and governance during the early years of Pakistan's new constitutional era. The article specified that the qualifications and disqualifications for membership in these assemblies would remain consistent with those outlined in the Interim Constitution, with a notable exception for members filling casual vacancies.

The Fourth Amendment in 1975 introduced significant changes to Article 273. It expanded the composition of the first Provincial Assemblies by allowing for the election of additional members to fill seats as specified in Article 106 of the Constitution. This amendment aimed to enhance representation within the assemblies and ensure that they reflected the broader demographic and political landscape of the provinces. The amendment also adjusted the qualifications and disqualifications criteria for these additional members, aligning them with the requirements of the Interim Constitution. These changes were likely driven by political considerations to accommodate evolving political dynamics and ensure a more inclusive legislative process.

Overall, Article 273 played a vital role in the early constitutional history of Pakistan by facilitating the transition to a stable legislative framework. The amendments made to this article reflected the need for adaptability in the face of changing political realities, ensuring that the Provincial Assemblies remained representative and functional during a critical period in Pakistan's democratic development.
