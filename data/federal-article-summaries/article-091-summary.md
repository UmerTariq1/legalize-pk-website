Article 91 of the Constitution of Pakistan, originally titled "The Prime Minister," outlines the process for electing the Prime Minister and the conditions under which they hold office. Initially, it established that the National Assembly would meet following a general election to elect a Prime Minister from among its Muslim members, requiring a majority vote. If no candidate achieved a majority in the first round, a runoff would occur between the top two candidates. The elected Prime Minister would then take an oath before the President.

The Eighth Amendment in 1985, during General Zia-ul-Haq's regime, introduced significant changes. It added a provision allowing the President to invite a member who commands the majority's confidence to become Prime Minister, reflecting a shift towards presidential discretion. This amendment also included a clause stating that the Prime Minister holds office at the President's pleasure, though the President could only act if the Prime Minister lost the Assembly's confidence. This change was part of broader efforts to strengthen presidential powers during Zia's rule.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, under President Asif Ali Zardari, redefined the article, renaming it "The Cabinet" and emphasizing parliamentary supremacy. It established a Cabinet led by the Prime Minister to advise the President, reduced the timeline for the National Assembly to convene post-election, and removed the President's discretionary power to appoint the Prime Minister. The amendment also introduced collective responsibility of the Cabinet to both houses of Parliament and clarified the conditions under which ministers could hold office. These changes aimed to restore parliamentary democracy and reduce presidential influence, reflecting a shift back towards a parliamentary system after years of military and presidential dominance.
