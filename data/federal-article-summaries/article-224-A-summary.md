Article 224-A of the Constitution of Pakistan was introduced through the Twentieth Amendment in 2012. This article establishes a mechanism for appointing a caretaker Prime Minister and caretaker Chief Ministers when the Prime Minister and the Leader of the Opposition, or their provincial counterparts, cannot agree on a candidate. The caretaker government is crucial in ensuring a neutral and fair environment for conducting general elections, as it takes over the administration from the outgoing government until a new government is elected.

The article outlines a structured process to resolve disagreements over caretaker appointments. If the Prime Minister and the Leader of the Opposition in the National Assembly cannot agree on a caretaker Prime Minister within three days of the assembly's dissolution, they must each propose two nominees to a specially formed committee. This committee, comprising equal members from the Treasury and the Opposition, is tasked with finalizing the appointment within three days. A similar process applies at the provincial level for appointing caretaker Chief Ministers. If the committee fails to reach a decision, the matter is escalated to the Election Commission of Pakistan, which must decide within two days. This process ensures that the caretaker appointments are made swiftly and with bipartisan input, reducing the potential for political bias.

The introduction of Article 224-A was significant because it provided a clear, time-bound procedure for appointing caretaker governments, which are essential for maintaining the integrity of the electoral process. By involving both the ruling party and the opposition in the decision-making process, the amendment aimed to enhance transparency and trust in the transition period leading up to elections. This mechanism also underscores the importance of the Election Commission as a neutral arbiter in cases where political consensus cannot be reached, thereby reinforcing its role in safeguarding democratic processes in Pakistan.
