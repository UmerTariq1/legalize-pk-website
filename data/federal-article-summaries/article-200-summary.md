Article 200 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the transfer of High Court judges between different High Courts. Initially, it empowered the President to transfer judges with their consent and after consulting the Chief Justice of Pakistan and the Chief Justices of the involved High Courts. This provision ensures that the judiciary remains flexible and can respond to varying workloads across different regions, while also safeguarding the independence of judges by requiring their consent and consultation with senior judicial figures.

Over time, this article has undergone several amendments, reflecting shifts in the balance of power and the evolving needs of the judiciary. The First Amendment in 1974 introduced a provision allowing temporary increases in the number of judges in a High Court, with the consent of the judge and necessary consultations, to manage workload fluctuations. The Fifth Amendment in 1976 allowed for transfers without consent for periods not exceeding one year, indicating a move towards greater executive flexibility in judicial administration. However, the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 removed this provision, reinstating the requirement for consent and consultation, likely in response to concerns about judicial independence.

The most significant changes came with the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, which shifted the power to recommend transfers from the President to the Judicial Commission of Pakistan, including the Chief Justices of the involved High Courts. This change aimed to enhance the role of the judiciary in its own administration, reducing executive influence. The amendment also introduced provisions regarding the seniority of transferred judges and the consequences for judges who refuse transfers, such as deemed retirement with pension benefits. These changes reflect a continued effort to balance judicial independence with administrative efficiency, ensuring that the judiciary can function effectively while maintaining its autonomy.
