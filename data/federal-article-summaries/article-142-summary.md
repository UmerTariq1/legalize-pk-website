Article 142 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the distribution of legislative powers between the federal and provincial governments. Initially, it established that Parliament had exclusive authority to legislate on matters listed in the Federal Legislative List, while both Parliament and Provincial Assemblies could legislate on matters in the Concurrent Legislative List. For matters not mentioned in either list, only Provincial Assemblies had the power to legislate, except for areas not included in any province, where Parliament had exclusive authority. This framework was designed to delineate the scope of legislative authority, ensuring a balance of power between the federal and provincial levels of government, which is crucial in a federal system like Pakistan's.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, significantly altered Article 142 by removing the Concurrent Legislative List, which previously allowed both federal and provincial governments to legislate on the same subjects. This amendment shifted more legislative power to the provinces, reflecting a move towards greater provincial autonomy. The amendment also specified that both the Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament) and Provincial Assemblies could legislate on criminal law, criminal procedure, and evidence, while maintaining Parliament's exclusive power over matters in the Federal Legislative List and areas not included in any province. This change was part of a broader effort to decentralize power and address longstanding demands for provincial rights, thereby impacting the governance structure by enhancing provincial legislative authority and reducing federal overreach.
