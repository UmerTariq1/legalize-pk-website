Article 111 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 originally established the right of the Advocate-General to participate in the proceedings of the Provincial Assembly and its committees. The Advocate-General, a legal advisor to the provincial government, was given the ability to speak and engage in discussions, although not to vote. This provision ensures that the legal perspective is considered in legislative discussions, contributing to informed decision-making and adherence to legal frameworks within the provincial legislative process.

The article underwent a significant change with the Twenty-sixth Amendment in 2024, which extended the same participatory rights to an Adviser appointed under clause (11) of Article 130. This amendment reflects a shift towards including more voices in the legislative process, particularly those of advisers who may have specialized knowledge or expertise relevant to provincial governance. By allowing both the Advocate-General and the Adviser to speak and participate, the amendment aims to enhance the quality of legislative debate and ensure that diverse perspectives are considered, although neither is granted voting rights. This change likely stems from a political desire to incorporate broader expertise and advisory input into legislative processes, reflecting an evolving approach to governance in Pakistan.
