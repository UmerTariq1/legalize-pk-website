Article 236 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the revocation of proclamations, which are formal declarations made by the government, often in times of emergency or significant national importance. This article establishes the legal framework for how such proclamations can be altered or nullified. Specifically, it allows for a proclamation to be varied or revoked by issuing a subsequent proclamation. This means that the government has the flexibility to adapt to changing circumstances by modifying or canceling previous declarations as needed.

Furthermore, Article 236 ensures that the validity of any proclamation or order issued under this part of the Constitution cannot be challenged in court. This provision is significant as it underscores the finality and authority of such proclamations, preventing judicial review or interference. This could be seen as a measure to maintain governmental stability and authority during critical times, ensuring that decisions made under emergency conditions are not subject to prolonged legal disputes.

The article has remained unchanged since its inception in 1973, indicating that its provisions have been deemed sufficient and effective in their original form. Its unaltered state suggests a consensus on the necessity of granting the government decisive power to manage emergencies without the hindrance of judicial challenges. This reflects a balance between the need for swift governmental action in times of crisis and the overarching principle of rule of law, albeit with limited judicial oversight in these specific circumstances.
