Article 29 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, outlines the "Principles of Policy," which serve as guiding principles for the governance of the country. These principles are intended to direct the actions of all state organs and authorities, ensuring that their functions align with the broader goals and values enshrined in the Constitution. The article emphasizes the responsibility of state organs and individuals acting on their behalf to adhere to these principles, although it acknowledges that their implementation may depend on the availability of resources. This provision underscores the aspirational nature of the Principles of Policy, recognizing that practical constraints may affect their full realization.

Originally, Article 29 required the President and the Governors of the provinces to prepare annual reports on the observance and implementation of these principles, which were to be presented to the National Assembly or the respective Provincial Assemblies. This requirement aimed to ensure accountability and provide a platform for legislative discussion on how well the principles were being followed. However, the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 brought a significant change to this process. It expanded the reporting requirement to include both Houses of the Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament), namely the National Assembly and the Senate, thereby enhancing parliamentary oversight and encouraging a more comprehensive discussion on the implementation of the Principles of Policy. This amendment reflects a broader effort to strengthen democratic processes and ensure that the principles guiding state policy are subject to scrutiny by a wider legislative audience.
