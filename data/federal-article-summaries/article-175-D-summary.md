Article 175-D, introduced through the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, establishes the requirement for the Chief Justice and other judges of the Federal Constitutional Court to take an oath of office. This article specifies that the Chief Justice must take the oath before the President of Pakistan, while other judges must take their oaths before the Chief Justice. The oath is to be in the form outlined in the Third Schedule of the Constitution. This provision is significant as it formalizes the process of assuming judicial office, ensuring that judges of the Federal Constitutional Court commit to upholding the Constitution and performing their duties with integrity and impartiality.

The introduction of this article reflects a structured approach to maintaining the independence and accountability of the judiciary, particularly within the newly established Federal Constitutional Court. By requiring an oath, the amendment underscores the importance of judicial responsibility and the ethical standards expected of those who interpret and uphold the Constitution. This change likely aimed to reinforce public confidence in the judiciary by ensuring that judges are bound by a formal commitment to their constitutional duties. The political context of this amendment, introduced during Asif Ali Zardari's influence, may suggest an effort to bolster the judicial framework in response to evolving legal and political challenges in Pakistan.
