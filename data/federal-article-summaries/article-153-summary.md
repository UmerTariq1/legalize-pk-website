Article 153 of the Constitution of Pakistan establishes the Council of Common Interests (CCI), a body designed to ensure cooperation and coordination between the federal and provincial governments. This council is crucial in Pakistan's federal structure as it addresses issues that require joint decision-making and oversight, such as resource distribution and policy implementation across provinces. Originally, the CCI was to be appointed by the President and included the Chief Ministers of the provinces and an equal number of members from the federal government, nominated by the Prime Minister. The Prime Minister, if a member, would serve as the Chairman, or otherwise, a federal minister could be nominated by the President to chair the council. The CCI was accountable to Parliament, reflecting its role in balancing federal and provincial interests.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 brought significant changes to this article. It redefined the composition of the CCI by making the Prime Minister the permanent Chairman of the Council, thereby centralizing leadership within the federal government. The amendment also specified that the Council would include the Chief Ministers of the provinces and three members from the federal government, rather than an equal number to the provincial representatives. This change streamlined the council's structure and potentially increased federal influence. Additionally, the amendment introduced a requirement for the CCI to submit an annual report to both Houses of Parliament, enhancing transparency and accountability. These changes were part of broader efforts to strengthen provincial autonomy while maintaining effective federal oversight, reflecting the political climate of the time which sought to address longstanding provincial grievances and improve governance.
