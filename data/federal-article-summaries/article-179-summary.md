Article 179 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 deals with the retirement age of judges of the Supreme Court. Initially, it established that a Supreme Court judge would serve until the age of sixty-five unless they resigned or were removed according to constitutional procedures. This provision is crucial as it ensures a fixed tenure for judges, promoting stability and predictability in the judiciary, which is a cornerstone of the rule of law and judicial independence.

The article has undergone several amendments reflecting changes in the political landscape and evolving views on judicial tenure. The Fifth Amendment in 1976 introduced a five-year term for the Chief Justice, allowing them to either retire with full pension or continue as the most senior judge, a move likely aimed at balancing continuity with the need for fresh leadership. The Sixth Amendment later that year allowed a Chief Justice to complete a five-year term even if they reached the age of sixty-five, ensuring experienced leadership could continue in office. However, the Seventeenth Amendment in 2003 reverted to the original provision, removing these additional clauses, possibly to simplify the retirement process and avoid potential conflicts over tenure extensions.

In recent years, the Twenty-sixth and Twenty-seventh Amendments in 2024 and 2025 respectively, further refined the terms for the Chief Justice, setting a fixed three-year term regardless of age, emphasizing a clear and predictable transition in leadership. These changes reflect ongoing efforts to balance judicial independence with the need for periodic renewal in the judiciary's leadership, ensuring that the highest court remains dynamic and responsive to the needs of justice in Pakistan.
