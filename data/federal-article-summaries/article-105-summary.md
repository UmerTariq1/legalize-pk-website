Article 105 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the role and responsibilities of a provincial Governor, particularly emphasizing the requirement for the Governor to act on the advice of the Chief Minister or the Cabinet. This article is crucial as it delineates the balance of power between the Governor, who is the ceremonial head of a province, and the elected Chief Minister, who is the executive authority. By mandating that the Governor act on the advice of the Chief Minister or Cabinet, the article ensures that the executive powers within a province are exercised in accordance with the democratic will as expressed through the elected representatives.

The article has undergone significant changes through constitutional amendments. Initially, in 1973, the Governor was required to act solely on the advice of the Chief Minister. However, the Eighth Amendment in 1985, during General Zia-ul-Haq's regime, expanded this to include advice from the Cabinet as well. This amendment also introduced a provision allowing the Governor to request a reconsideration of the advice, although ultimately, the Governor was bound to follow the advice after reconsideration. This change reflected a shift towards a more collective executive decision-making process, potentially diluting the power of the Chief Minister alone.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 further refined these provisions by setting specific time frames for the reconsideration process, thereby adding clarity and efficiency to the decision-making process. Additionally, this amendment introduced a new clause that provided for the Governor's role in dissolving the Provincial Assembly and managing the transition to a caretaker government, ensuring continuity and stability in governance. These changes were part of broader efforts to strengthen democratic institutions and processes in Pakistan, reflecting a move towards greater provincial autonomy and accountability.
