Article 114 of the Constitution of Pakistan, established in 1973, is designed to maintain the independence and integrity of the judiciary by restricting discussions in Provincial Assemblies about the conduct of judges. Specifically, it prohibits any debate regarding the conduct of judges from the Supreme Court or High Courts in the execution of their duties. This provision is crucial as it safeguards the judiciary from political pressures and influences, ensuring that judges can perform their roles without fear of political retribution or interference. By limiting such discussions, the article aims to uphold the separation of powers, a fundamental principle in democratic governance, which helps maintain a balance between the legislative and judicial branches of government.

The article underwent a significant change with the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025. This amendment expanded the scope of the restriction to include judges of the newly established Federal Constitutional Court, alongside those of the Supreme Court and High Courts. The practical effect of this amendment was to extend the same protections and independence to judges of the Federal Constitutional Court, reflecting the evolving judicial structure in Pakistan. The inclusion of the Federal Constitutional Court indicates a response to the changing legal landscape and possibly an effort to address new judicial challenges or disputes that may arise at a constitutional level. This change underscores the ongoing commitment to preserving judicial independence across all levels of the judiciary, which is essential for maintaining public confidence in the legal system and ensuring the rule of law.
