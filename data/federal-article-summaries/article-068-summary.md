Article 68 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 establishes a restriction on parliamentary discussions concerning the conduct of judges. Initially, this article prohibited any discussion in Parliament about the conduct of judges from the Supreme Court or High Courts while they were performing their duties. This provision serves to maintain the independence and impartiality of the judiciary by preventing political interference or undue influence from the legislative branch. It underscores the separation of powers, a fundamental principle in democratic governance, ensuring that judges can perform their duties without fear of political retribution or pressure.

The article underwent a significant change with the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025. This amendment expanded the scope of the restriction to include judges of the newly established Federal Constitutional Court, alongside those of the Supreme Court and High Courts. The inclusion of the Federal Constitutional Court reflects an evolution in Pakistan's judicial structure, possibly indicating the creation of a new judicial body tasked with constitutional matters. This change underscores the importance of protecting the integrity and independence of all judicial bodies, not just the traditional courts, from parliamentary scrutiny. The amendment likely aimed to adapt the constitutional framework to new judicial realities while maintaining the core principle of judicial independence.
