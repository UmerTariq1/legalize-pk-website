Article 23 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 establishes the right of every citizen to acquire, hold, and dispose of property anywhere in Pakistan. This article is significant as it enshrines a fundamental economic right, ensuring that individuals have the freedom to own and manage property, which is a cornerstone of personal and economic liberty. The provision is crucial for fostering economic development and personal autonomy, as it allows citizens to engage in transactions and investments that can lead to wealth creation and economic stability.

However, the article also acknowledges that this right is not absolute and can be subject to "reasonable restrictions imposed by law in the public interest." This clause allows the government to regulate property rights to balance individual freedoms with broader societal needs, such as urban planning, environmental protection, or national security. The inclusion of this provision reflects a recognition that while property rights are fundamental, they must be harmonized with the collective interests of the community.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 23 has not been amended, indicating a consistent recognition of the importance of property rights within the constitutional framework of Pakistan. The stability of this article suggests that the balance it strikes between individual rights and public interest has been deemed appropriate and effective by successive governments and lawmakers. This stability is crucial for maintaining public confidence in property rights, which are essential for economic activity and investment.
