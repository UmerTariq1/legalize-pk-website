Article 167 of the Constitution of Pakistan deals with the borrowing powers of provincial governments. Initially established in the 1973 Constitution, this article outlines the conditions under which a province can borrow money. It allows provinces to borrow against their Provincial Consolidated Fund, provided they adhere to limits set by their respective Provincial Assemblies. Additionally, the Federal Government has the authority to lend money to provinces or guarantee loans, provided these do not exceed limits set under Article 166. However, provinces cannot raise loans without federal consent if they have outstanding federal loans or guarantees.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, introduced a significant change to Article 167. It added a provision allowing provinces to raise domestic or international loans and give guarantees on the security of their Provincial Consolidated Fund. This new power is subject to conditions specified by the National Economic Council. The amendment aimed to enhance provincial autonomy by giving provinces more control over their financial matters, reflecting a broader political movement towards decentralization and empowering provincial governments. This change has practical implications, as it allows provinces to independently manage their financial needs and development projects, potentially leading to more tailored and effective economic strategies at the provincial level.
