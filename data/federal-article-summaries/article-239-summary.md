Article 239 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, outlines the procedure for amending the Constitution. This article is crucial as it establishes the formal process through which constitutional changes can be made, ensuring that such changes are deliberate and reflect a broad consensus. The process begins in the National Assembly, where a proposed amendment must receive the support of at least two-thirds of its total membership. Once passed, the Bill is sent to the Senate, where it must also secure a majority vote. If the Senate suggests amendments, the Bill returns to the National Assembly for reconsideration, requiring the same two-thirds majority to pass the amended version. This bicameral legislative process ensures that both houses of Parliament have a role in constitutional amendments, reflecting a balance of power and preventing hasty changes.

The article also specifies the role of the President in the amendment process. Once both houses pass the Bill, it is presented to the President for assent. The President must give assent within seven days, and if not, the assent is automatically deemed granted after this period. This provision ensures that the President cannot indefinitely delay constitutional amendments, maintaining the legislative authority of Parliament.

Additionally, Article 239 includes a special provision for amendments that would alter the boundaries of a province. Such amendments require not only the usual parliamentary approval but also a two-thirds majority vote in the provincial assembly of the affected province. This clause protects the interests of provinces by giving them a decisive voice in matters directly affecting their territorial integrity. Overall, Article 239 is fundamental in maintaining the stability and integrity of the constitutional framework by setting a high threshold for amendments, thereby safeguarding against arbitrary or politically motivated changes.
