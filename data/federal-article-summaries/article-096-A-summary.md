Article 96-A of the Constitution of Pakistan, introduced through the Seventh Amendment in 1977, establishes a mechanism for the Prime Minister to seek a vote of confidence from the people of Pakistan through a referendum. This article was added during the tenure of Prime Minister Fazal Ilahi Chaudhry, a period marked by political instability and the need for legitimizing leadership amidst challenges to authority. The purpose of this article is to provide a democratic tool for the Prime Minister to directly engage with the electorate, thereby reinforcing or questioning the mandate to govern.

The article outlines the process whereby the Prime Minister can advise the President to initiate a referendum, which is then conducted by a Referendum Commission. The law governing this process is to be established by Parliament, ensuring that the referendum is carried out in an organized and lawful manner. The article also specifies that any disputes regarding the referendum's vote counting are to be resolved by the Referendum Commission, and such disputes cannot be contested in any court, thus centralizing the authority and finality of the referendum process.

The practical effect of this article is significant as it provides a constitutional avenue for the Prime Minister to either reaffirm their leadership or, if unsuccessful in securing a majority vote, to step down, as per the stipulations of Article 94. This mechanism underscores the importance of public confidence in democratic governance and offers a direct method for the electorate to express their support or lack thereof for the sitting Prime Minister. The introduction of this article reflects the political context of the time, aiming to stabilize governance by directly involving the populace in critical decisions about leadership legitimacy.
