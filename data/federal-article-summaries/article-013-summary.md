Article 13 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is a fundamental safeguard designed to protect individuals from two specific legal injustices: double jeopardy and self-incrimination. Double jeopardy refers to the principle that no person should be prosecuted or punished more than once for the same offense. This protection ensures that once a person has been tried and either acquitted or convicted, they cannot be subjected to another trial for the same crime, thereby providing finality and certainty in legal proceedings. Self-incrimination protection means that an individual accused of a crime cannot be forced to testify against themselves, which upholds the principle that the burden of proof lies with the prosecution and not the defendant.

This article is crucial in maintaining the integrity of the legal system and protecting individual rights within Pakistan's constitutional framework. It ensures fairness in legal proceedings and prevents the abuse of state power by safeguarding individuals from repeated prosecutions and coercive interrogation practices. Since its inception in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 13 has remained unchanged, reflecting its foundational role in the protection of civil liberties. Its unamended status underscores the enduring importance of these protections in the legal and constitutional landscape of Pakistan, ensuring that citizens are treated justly and their rights are preserved in the face of criminal accusations.
