Article 143 of the Constitution of Pakistan addresses the issue of inconsistencies between federal and provincial laws. Its primary purpose is to establish a clear hierarchy where federal laws take precedence over provincial laws in cases of conflict. This is crucial for maintaining a unified legal framework across the country, ensuring that provincial legislation does not contradict or undermine federal laws. The article ensures that if a provincial assembly enacts a law that conflicts with a federal law, the federal law will prevail, and the conflicting provincial law will be considered void to the extent of the inconsistency. This provision is significant in maintaining the balance of power between the federal and provincial governments and ensuring that national interests are not compromised by regional legislation.

The article was originally included in the 1973 Constitution and remained unchanged until the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. The amendment did not alter the fundamental principle of federal supremacy but made a notable change in terminology. It replaced the term "Parliament" with "Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament)," aligning the language with the official nomenclature used in the Constitution. This change reflects a broader effort during the Eighteenth Amendment to update and clarify constitutional language without altering the substantive legal principles. The practical effect of this amendment was minimal in terms of legal impact but was part of a larger constitutional reform aimed at decentralizing power and enhancing provincial autonomy while maintaining the necessary federal oversight.
