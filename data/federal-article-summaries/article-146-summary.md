Article 146 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the framework for the Federal Government to delegate its powers to the provincial governments under certain conditions. This article is significant as it provides a mechanism for the central government to entrust specific functions to provincial authorities, thereby promoting administrative flexibility and cooperation between different levels of government. The article allows the Federal Government to delegate responsibilities to provincial governments or their officers, either conditionally or unconditionally, with the consent of the provincial government. This delegation can occur even if the provincial assembly does not have the legislative power over the matter in question, as long as it is within the executive authority of the Federation.

The article also addresses the financial implications of such delegation. It stipulates that if the delegation of powers results in additional administrative costs for the province, the Federation is required to compensate the province. The amount of compensation is to be mutually agreed upon, or if an agreement cannot be reached, it is to be determined by an arbitrator appointed by the Chief Justice of Pakistan. This provision ensures that provinces are not financially burdened by the additional responsibilities conferred upon them by the Federation.

Since its inception in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 146 has not been amended. Its enduring presence underscores its importance in facilitating intergovernmental relations and ensuring that the distribution of powers and responsibilities between the Federation and the provinces can be adjusted as needed to address administrative and governance challenges. This flexibility is crucial in a federal system like Pakistan's, where balancing central authority with provincial autonomy is a constant and evolving challenge.
