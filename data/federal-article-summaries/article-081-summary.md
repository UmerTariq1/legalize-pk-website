Article 81 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the expenditures that are charged upon the Federal Consolidated Fund, which is essentially the government's central account for managing its finances. This article is crucial because it specifies which governmental expenses are automatically funded without requiring annual approval from the legislature. These include the salaries and related expenses of high-ranking officials such as the President, judges of the Supreme Court, and other key positions, as well as administrative costs for significant institutions like the Supreme Court and the Election Commission. Additionally, it covers debt obligations and any legal judgments against Pakistan, ensuring that these critical financial commitments are met consistently.

Over the years, Article 81 has been amended several times to reflect changes in Pakistan's judicial and administrative structure. The Nineteenth Amendment in 2011 expanded the scope to include the Islamabad High Court, reflecting its establishment and the need to ensure its financial independence. The Twenty-second Amendment in 2016 made a minor but significant change by clarifying the terminology regarding staff remuneration. The Twenty-sixth Amendment in 2024 further broadened the article to include the Judicial Commission of Pakistan and the Supreme Judicial Council, and it introduced provisions for funding elections, highlighting the growing complexity and importance of electoral processes in Pakistan.

The most recent change, the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, introduced the Federal Constitutional Court into the list of institutions whose expenses are charged to the Federal Consolidated Fund. This reflects an ongoing evolution in Pakistan's judicial framework, possibly in response to political or administrative needs for a more specialized constitutional judiciary. These amendments collectively underscore the dynamic nature of Pakistan's constitutional framework, adapting to new institutional developments and ensuring that critical state functions remain financially secure.
