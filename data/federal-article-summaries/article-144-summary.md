Article 144 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the power of the federal Parliament to legislate on matters that are not specifically listed in the Constitution's Fourth Schedule, provided that two or more Provincial Assemblies consent to such legislation. This article is significant because it allows for a collaborative legislative process between the federal and provincial governments, ensuring that certain issues can be addressed uniformly across multiple provinces when they agree on the necessity of such federal intervention. This mechanism is crucial for maintaining a balance of power and cooperation between different levels of government, allowing for flexibility in governance and addressing issues that may require a broader legislative approach than what individual provinces can provide.

The article has undergone changes through constitutional amendments, reflecting shifts in the political landscape and governance philosophy. Initially, the article required the consent of two or more Provincial Assemblies for Parliament to legislate on matters not listed in the Fourth Schedule. This was slightly altered by the Eighth Amendment in 1985, which did not change the substance of the article but updated its documentation. The more significant change came with the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, which reduced the requirement from two or more Provincial Assemblies to just one or more. This change expanded the scope for federal legislative intervention, making it easier for the Parliament to address issues that might affect only one province but still require federal oversight or uniformity. This amendment was part of a broader effort to decentralize power and enhance provincial autonomy while still allowing for necessary federal legislative action when provinces deem it appropriate.
