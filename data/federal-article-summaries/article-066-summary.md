Article 66 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 establishes the privileges and immunities of members of Parliament. Its primary purpose is to ensure that members of Parliament can perform their legislative duties without fear of legal repercussions for their speeches or votes within Parliament. This protection is crucial for maintaining the independence and effectiveness of the legislative process, allowing members to speak freely and make decisions based on their judgment without external pressures or threats of litigation. The article also extends these privileges to the publication of parliamentary reports and proceedings, ensuring transparency and accountability while safeguarding the legislative process from undue interference.

The article further outlines that the powers, immunities, and privileges of Parliament and its members can be defined by law. Until such laws are enacted, the privileges remain as they were before the Constitution's commencement. This provision allows for flexibility and adaptation over time, ensuring that parliamentary privileges can evolve with changing legal and political contexts. Additionally, the article provides for the punishment of individuals who refuse to cooperate with parliamentary committees, highlighting the importance of accountability and the ability of Parliament to conduct thorough investigations.

Importantly, Article 66 applies not only to members of Parliament but also to other individuals who have the right to participate in parliamentary proceedings. This broad application underscores the article's role in protecting the integrity and functionality of the legislative process as a whole. By safeguarding the freedom of speech within Parliament and outlining the framework for parliamentary privileges, Article 66 plays a vital role in upholding democratic principles and ensuring that the legislative branch can operate effectively and independently within Pakistan's constitutional framework.
