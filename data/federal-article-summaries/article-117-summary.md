Article 117 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the continuity of legislative processes within Provincial Assemblies, specifically concerning the status of bills during periods of prorogation and dissolution. The purpose of this article is to ensure that legislative work is not unduly disrupted by the temporary suspension of assembly sessions, known as prorogation. It establishes that a bill under consideration in a Provincial Assembly will not be discarded or terminated simply because the assembly session has been temporarily ended. This provision is crucial for maintaining legislative efficiency and continuity, allowing assemblies to resume consideration of bills without starting from scratch after a break.

However, the article also stipulates that if the Provincial Assembly is dissolved, any bill that has not been passed will lapse. This means that the legislative process for that bill must begin anew when a new assembly is formed. This rule underscores the principle that a new assembly, potentially with different representatives and priorities, should have the opportunity to reconsider pending legislation. The distinction between prorogation and dissolution is significant because it balances the need for legislative continuity with the democratic principle of allowing newly elected assemblies to set their legislative agenda.

Since its inception in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 117 has not been amended. This stability suggests that the provisions effectively balance the need for legislative continuity with democratic renewal, reflecting a consensus on how legislative processes should be managed in the context of assembly sessions and elections. The article plays a vital role in ensuring that the legislative process in Pakistan's provincial governments is both efficient and responsive to the democratic will of the electorate.
