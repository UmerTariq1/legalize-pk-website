Article 228 of the Constitution of Pakistan establishes the Council of Islamic Ideology, commonly referred to as the Islamic Council. This body is tasked with advising the government on whether laws are in accordance with Islamic principles as outlined in the Quran and Sunnah. The Council plays a crucial role in ensuring that legislation aligns with Islamic values, which is significant in a country where Islam is the state religion. The composition of the Council is designed to include a diverse range of Islamic scholars and experts in various fields, ensuring that multiple perspectives within Islamic thought are represented. This diversity is intended to provide comprehensive and balanced advice to the government.

Originally, the article mandated that the Council consist of between eight and fifteen members, appointed by the President, with specific qualifications. These included having knowledge of Islamic principles or expertise in Pakistan's economic, political, legal, or administrative issues. The President was required to ensure representation from various schools of thought, include at least two judges from the Supreme Court or High Court, and appoint at least four members with extensive experience in Islamic research or instruction. Additionally, the inclusion of at least one female member was mandated, reflecting an early recognition of gender diversity.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 brought a significant change to the composition criteria of the Council. It altered the requirement from having at least four members with fifteen years of experience in Islamic research or instruction to having at least one-third of the members meet this criterion. This change potentially increased the number of members with such expertise, thereby strengthening the Council's capacity to provide informed and scholarly advice. The amendment reflects an ongoing effort to adapt the Council's structure to better fulfill its advisory role in a changing socio-political landscape.
