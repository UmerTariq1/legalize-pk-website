Article 62 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the qualifications required for an individual to be elected or chosen as a member of the Parliament, known as the Majlis-e-Shoora. Initially established in the 1973 Constitution, this article sets forth basic criteria such as citizenship, age, and voter registration requirements for both the National Assembly and the Senate. It is a crucial component of the constitutional framework as it ensures that those who represent the people in the legislative process meet certain standards of eligibility, thereby maintaining the integrity and functionality of the parliamentary system.

The article has undergone significant changes through constitutional amendments. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 expanded the qualifications to include moral and religious criteria, such as being of good character, having adequate knowledge of Islamic teachings, and being honest and righteous. These additions reflect an emphasis on the moral and ethical standards expected of parliamentarians, aligning with the Islamic principles that underpin much of Pakistan's legal framework. The amendment also clarified the qualifications for women and non-Muslims, ensuring inclusivity in the electoral process.

The Twenty-fifth Amendment in 2018 made a specific change by removing the Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA) from the voter registration requirements for the Senate, following the merger of FATA with Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province. This change was part of a broader effort to integrate FATA into the national political and administrative structure, reflecting the evolving political landscape of Pakistan. Overall, the amendments to Article 62 highlight the dynamic nature of constitutional law in Pakistan, adapting to political, social, and religious considerations over time.
