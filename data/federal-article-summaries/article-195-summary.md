Article 195 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 originally set the retirement age for judges of the High Court at sixty-two years. This provision was crucial as it established a clear tenure for High Court judges, ensuring a balance between judicial independence and accountability. The retirement age serves to maintain a dynamic and evolving judiciary, allowing for the infusion of new perspectives while ensuring experienced judges serve for a substantial period.

The article underwent significant changes with the Fifth and Sixth Amendments in 1976. The Fifth Amendment introduced a specific term for the Chief Justice of a High Court, allowing them to hold office for four years or until the age of sixty-two, whichever came first. It also provided the Chief Justice with options upon completing their term, such as retiring with a pension or continuing as the most senior judge. This amendment aimed to address concerns about the concentration of power and ensure a smooth transition in judicial leadership. The Sixth Amendment further refined this by allowing a Chief Justice who had not completed five years in office by the age of sixty-two to continue until the term was completed, thus providing stability and continuity in the judiciary.

However, the Seventeenth Amendment in 2003 reversed these changes, simplifying the article back to its original form where a High Court judge, including the Chief Justice, would retire at sixty-two years. This rollback might have been influenced by political considerations to streamline judicial appointments and avoid complexities in the tenure of Chief Justices. The practical effect of these amendments and their reversal was to oscillate between providing flexibility in judicial tenure and maintaining a straightforward retirement age, reflecting the ongoing balancing act between judicial independence and administrative clarity in Pakistan's constitutional framework.
