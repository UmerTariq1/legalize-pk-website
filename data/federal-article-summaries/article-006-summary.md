Article 6 of the Constitution of Pakistan is a crucial provision that addresses the crime of high treason. Initially introduced in the 1973 Constitution under the leadership of Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, this article establishes that any individual who attempts to overthrow, subvert, or conspire against the Constitution through force or other unconstitutional means is guilty of high treason. It also extends this culpability to those who aid or abet such actions. The article mandates that Parliament must legislate the punishment for those found guilty of high treason, underscoring the seriousness with which the Constitution treats any threat to its integrity and the democratic framework of the state.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010 during Asif Ali Zardari's presidency, expanded the scope of Article 6. It introduced additional terms such as "suspends" and "holds in abeyance" to the list of actions considered high treason, reflecting a broader understanding of threats to constitutional order. This amendment also added a clause stating that acts of high treason cannot be validated by any court, including the Supreme Court and High Courts. This change was significant as it aimed to prevent any judicial endorsement of unconstitutional acts, a response to historical instances where courts had previously validated military coups. By reinforcing the inviolability of the Constitution, these amendments sought to strengthen democratic governance and the rule of law in Pakistan.
