Article 63A of the Constitution of Pakistan addresses the disqualification of members of Parliament on the grounds of defection. This article is crucial as it seeks to maintain party discipline and stability within the parliamentary system by preventing elected representatives from switching allegiances for personal or political gain. It establishes the conditions under which a member of a parliamentary party may be disqualified for defecting, such as resigning from their party, joining another party, or voting against party directives on key issues like the election of the Prime Minister or Chief Minister, votes of confidence or no-confidence, and significant legislative bills.

The article was first introduced through the Fourteenth Amendment in 1997, which laid down the initial framework for handling defection. It provided a mechanism for party heads to initiate disqualification proceedings against defecting members, with the final decision resting with the party head and the Chief Election Commissioner. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 refined this process by involving the Election Commission more directly in confirming declarations of defection and allowing for an appeal process to the Supreme Court. This amendment aimed to ensure a more impartial and structured approach to handling defections, reflecting a shift towards greater judicial oversight.

The most recent change came with the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, which altered the appeal process by transferring jurisdiction from the Supreme Court to a newly established Federal Constitutional Court. This change likely reflects a political decision to streamline constitutional matters and provide a specialized forum for such appeals, potentially expediting the resolution of defection cases. Overall, Article 63A has evolved to balance the need for party discipline with procedural fairness, impacting the political landscape by discouraging opportunistic shifts in party loyalty among legislators.
