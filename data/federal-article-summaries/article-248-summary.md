Article 248 of the Constitution of Pakistan provides legal immunity to certain high-ranking officials, including the President, Governors, the Prime Minister, and various ministers, from being answerable to courts for actions taken in the exercise of their official powers. This article is significant because it establishes a legal shield for these officials, ensuring that they can perform their duties without the constant threat of litigation. The rationale behind such immunity is to allow these officials to make decisions and take actions in the interest of governance without fear of personal legal consequences, thereby facilitating the smooth functioning of the executive branch.

Originally, Article 248, as enacted in 1973, provided that these officials were not answerable to any court for their official acts and that no criminal or civil proceedings could be initiated against the President or a Governor during their term of office. This provision was designed to protect the highest offices in the country from legal entanglements that could impede their ability to govern effectively.

The Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025 introduced significant changes to this article. It added a clause stating that the President would not be answerable to any court for life, extending the immunity beyond their term of office. This amendment effectively grants lifelong immunity to the President from criminal proceedings and arrest, which was a notable shift from the original provision that limited such immunity to the duration of their term. The amendment reflects a political decision likely aimed at ensuring that former Presidents are not subjected to legal challenges that could arise from their time in office, thus providing them with a permanent safeguard. This change could be seen as controversial, as it raises questions about accountability and the balance of power within the government.
