Article 226 of the Constitution of Pakistan establishes the principle that all elections conducted under the Constitution should be by secret ballot, with the notable exceptions of the elections for the Prime Minister and the Chief Minister. This article underscores the importance of confidentiality in the electoral process, ensuring that voters can make their choices freely without fear of coercion or reprisal. The secret ballot is a fundamental aspect of democratic elections, as it protects the integrity of the voting process and upholds the principle of free and fair elections, which are crucial for the functioning of a democratic society.

The article was originally included in the 1973 Constitution under the leadership of Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto. It remained unchanged until the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, which was enacted during the presidency of Asif Ali Zardari. The amendment made a minor textual change by altering the title from "Elections to be by secret ballot" to "Election by secret ballot" and corrected a grammatical detail by changing "a Chief Minister" to "the Chief Minister." While these changes were largely editorial and did not alter the substantive content of the article, they reflect a broader effort during the Eighteenth Amendment to refine and update the language of the Constitution. The practical effect of these changes was minimal, as the core principle of conducting elections by secret ballot remained intact, continuing to safeguard the democratic process in Pakistan.
