Article 75 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the process by which a bill becomes law through the President's assent. Initially, in the 1973 Constitution, the President was required to assent to a bill within seven days of its presentation, after which the bill would automatically become law if no action was taken. This article is crucial as it establishes the procedural step necessary for legislative enactments to become binding law, ensuring a check on legislative processes while also preventing undue delays in the enactment of laws.

The article has undergone significant changes through constitutional amendments. The Eighth Amendment in 1985, during the regime of General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq, extended the period for presidential assent from seven to thirty days. This amendment also introduced a provision allowing the President to return a bill to the Parliament for reconsideration, which, if passed again by a majority, must be assented to by the President. This change reflected a shift towards increasing the President's role in legislative processes, likely influenced by the political context of Zia's military rule, which sought to centralize power.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, under President Asif Ali Zardari, reduced the period for presidential assent to ten days, reflecting a move towards expediting legislative processes and reducing executive delays. It also mandated that if a bill is returned and passed again by Parliament, the President must assent within ten days, or it will be deemed assented. This amendment was part of broader efforts to restore parliamentary supremacy and reduce the President's discretionary powers, aligning with democratic principles and addressing the imbalance created by previous amendments. These changes have had practical effects on the legislative process, ensuring a more efficient and less obstructive path for bills to become law.
