Article 57 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the right of certain key government officials to participate in parliamentary proceedings. Specifically, it grants the Prime Minister, Federal Ministers, Ministers of State, and the Attorney General the right to speak and engage in the activities of either House of Parliament, joint sittings, or any committees they are part of. However, this article explicitly states that these officials do not have the right to vote in these proceedings solely based on this provision.

The purpose of this article is to ensure that these high-ranking officials can contribute to legislative discussions and provide necessary insights or clarifications during parliamentary sessions. This is crucial for the functioning of a parliamentary democracy, as it allows the executive branch to communicate directly with the legislative branch, facilitating informed decision-making and legislative processes. By allowing these officials to speak but not vote, the article maintains a balance between enabling executive input and preserving the legislative body's autonomy in decision-making.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 57 has not been amended, indicating that its provisions have been deemed appropriate and effective in maintaining the intended balance between executive participation and legislative independence. This stability suggests that the article has successfully fulfilled its role in the constitutional framework of Pakistan, ensuring that key government figures can engage in parliamentary discourse without overstepping the boundaries of legislative authority.
