Article 177 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the process for appointing judges to the Supreme Court, which is the highest judicial authority in the country. The article is crucial as it establishes the criteria and procedure for selecting individuals who will interpret and uphold the Constitution and laws of Pakistan. Originally, the Chief Justice and other judges were appointed by the President, with the Chief Justice being consulted for other appointments. This process was intended to ensure that the judiciary remained independent and competent, as the judges play a pivotal role in maintaining the rule of law and protecting citizens' rights.

The article has undergone several amendments, reflecting changes in the political and legal landscape of Pakistan. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 was a significant change, as it introduced Article 175A, which altered the appointment process to involve a judicial commission and a parliamentary committee, thereby reducing the President's unilateral power in these appointments. This change aimed to enhance transparency and reduce political influence in judicial appointments. The Twenty-sixth Amendment in 2024 further refined the qualifications for Supreme Court judges, requiring that an advocate must also be an advocate of the Supreme Court, thus raising the bar for legal experience and expertise.

The most recent change, the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, made a minor linguistic adjustment by specifying the title "Chief Justice of Supreme Court" instead of "Chief Justice of Pakistan," which may reflect an effort to standardize terminology. These amendments collectively demonstrate an ongoing effort to balance the independence of the judiciary with accountability and to ensure that the process of appointing judges is robust and fair, which is vital for maintaining public confidence in the judicial system.
