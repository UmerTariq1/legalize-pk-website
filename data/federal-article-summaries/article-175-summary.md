Article 175 of the Constitution of Pakistan establishes the framework for the country's judicial system. Initially, it set up the Supreme Court of Pakistan, a High Court for each province, and allowed for the establishment of other courts by law. This article is crucial as it delineates the structure and jurisdiction of the judiciary, ensuring that courts operate within the powers conferred by the Constitution or other laws. It also mandated the separation of the judiciary from the executive, which is a fundamental principle for maintaining judicial independence and upholding the rule of law.

Over the years, Article 175 has undergone several amendments reflecting the evolving judicial needs of Pakistan. The Fifth Amendment in 1976 extended the timeline for separating the judiciary from the executive from three to five years, indicating a delay in achieving this separation, possibly due to administrative or political challenges. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 introduced the Islamabad High Court, recognizing the administrative significance of the Islamabad Capital Territory. The Nineteenth Amendment in 2011 clarified the inclusion of the Islamabad High Court within the constitutional framework, ensuring its jurisdiction was on par with provincial High Courts.

The Twenty-first and Twenty-third Amendments introduced provisions related to the trial of individuals associated with terrorist activities, reflecting Pakistan's ongoing struggle with terrorism and the need for specialized judicial processes. The Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025 marked a significant change by establishing a Federal Constitutional Court, indicating a shift towards a more specialized judicial body to handle constitutional matters, likely in response to increasing complexities in constitutional law. These amendments collectively show a trajectory towards refining the judicial system to better address both administrative needs and security challenges.
