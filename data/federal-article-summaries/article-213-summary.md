Article 213 of the Constitution of Pakistan establishes the position of the Chief Election Commissioner (CEC), a crucial role in overseeing the conduct of elections in the country. The CEC is responsible for ensuring free and fair elections, which is fundamental to the democratic process. Initially, the article specified that the CEC would be appointed by the President and must be a current or former judge of the Supreme Court or a High Court, ensuring that the appointee had a strong legal background.

Over time, Article 213 has undergone several amendments to refine the appointment process and broaden the eligibility criteria for the CEC. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 introduced a more collaborative appointment process, requiring the Prime Minister to consult with the Leader of the Opposition and forward three names to a Parliamentary Committee for confirmation. This change aimed to enhance the transparency and bipartisan nature of the appointment process. The Nineteenth Amendment in 2011 further clarified the composition of the Parliamentary Committee, ensuring equal representation from the Treasury and Opposition and specifying its size and composition during the dissolution of the National Assembly.

The Twenty-second Amendment in 2016 significantly expanded the pool of eligible candidates for the CEC by allowing senior civil servants and technocrats to be considered, in addition to judges. This amendment aimed to bring diverse expertise to the role, potentially improving the administration of elections. It also set an age limit of 68 years for the appointee, ensuring that the CEC is someone with both experience and the capacity to handle the demands of the position. These amendments reflect an ongoing effort to adapt the electoral oversight mechanism to the evolving political landscape of Pakistan, aiming to strengthen the integrity and effectiveness of its democratic processes.
