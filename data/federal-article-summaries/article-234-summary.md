Article 234 of the Constitution of Pakistan addresses the power of the President to issue a Proclamation in the event of a failure of constitutional machinery in a province. This article is significant as it provides a mechanism for the federal government to intervene in provincial matters when a province is unable to function according to constitutional provisions. This is crucial for maintaining the rule of law and ensuring that provincial governance aligns with the national constitution. The article allows the President to assume control of provincial functions, either directly or through the provincial governor, and to exercise the powers of the provincial assembly through the national Parliament. However, it explicitly prohibits the President from assuming judicial powers vested in the High Courts, thereby maintaining judicial independence.

The original text of Article 234, as enacted in 1973, allowed the President to act on a report from the provincial governor or otherwise, and required a joint sitting of Parliament to pass a resolution for such a proclamation. The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, refined this process by specifying that the resolution must be passed by each House of Parliament separately, rather than in a joint sitting. This change was part of a broader effort to enhance provincial autonomy and ensure a more balanced distribution of power between the federal and provincial governments. The amendment reflects a political shift towards greater decentralization and a more participatory legislative process, requiring both the National Assembly and the Senate to agree on the need for federal intervention in provincial affairs. This change has practical implications for how and when federal authority can be exercised over provinces, potentially making it more challenging for the central government to impose its will without broader legislative consensus.
