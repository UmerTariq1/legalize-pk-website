Article 71 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 originally outlined the legislative process for bills concerning matters listed in Part II of the Federal Legislative List or the Concurrent Legislative List. This article established the procedure for how such bills could be introduced and passed in the Parliament. It allowed a bill to originate in either the National Assembly or the Senate and required it to be transmitted to the other house for approval. If both houses passed the bill without amendments, it would be sent to the President for assent. However, if the second house rejected the bill, failed to pass it within ninety days, or passed it with amendments, the originating house could request a joint sitting of both houses to consider the bill. If the joint sitting approved the bill, it would then be presented to the President for assent. This process was significant as it ensured a collaborative legislative process between the two houses of Parliament, reflecting a system of checks and balances.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, significantly altered Article 71 by omitting it entirely from the Constitution. This amendment was part of a broader effort to decentralize power and enhance provincial autonomy by removing the Concurrent Legislative List, which previously allowed both federal and provincial governments to legislate on the same matters. The omission of Article 71 effectively removed the specific legislative process for bills related to the Concurrent Legislative List, as this list was abolished. The practical effect of this change was to streamline legislative powers and reduce the overlap between federal and provincial jurisdictions, thereby empowering provinces with greater legislative authority over their affairs. This shift was politically motivated, aiming to address longstanding demands for greater provincial autonomy and to strengthen the federal structure of Pakistan.
