Article 253 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the authority of the Parliament to set maximum limits on property ownership and control, as well as to regulate certain trades, businesses, industries, or services. Specifically, it allows the Parliament to legislate limits on the amount or type of property that individuals can own or control. This provision is significant as it reflects the state's ability to intervene in property rights to ensure equitable distribution and prevent excessive accumulation of wealth by individuals, which could lead to socio-economic disparities.

Additionally, the article empowers the Parliament to declare that certain economic activities may be exclusively conducted by the government or government-controlled corporations. This clause is crucial for maintaining state control over key sectors of the economy, which can be important for national security, economic stability, or public welfare. It reflects a balance between private enterprise and state intervention, allowing the government to step in where necessary to manage resources or services deemed essential for the public good.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 253 has not been amended. This suggests that the original provisions have remained relevant and effective in addressing the issues they were designed to tackle. The article's unaltered status indicates a continued acceptance of the need for legislative oversight in property ownership and economic activities, ensuring that these areas align with national interests and social justice objectives.
