Article 10 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the safeguards related to arrest and detention, ensuring that individuals are protected from arbitrary detention and have the right to legal representation. This article is crucial as it establishes the fundamental rights of individuals who are arrested, mandating that they be informed promptly of the reasons for their arrest and be allowed to consult a lawyer of their choice. It also requires that arrested individuals be presented before a magistrate within 24 hours, ensuring judicial oversight of detentions. However, the article also acknowledges the state's power to enact laws for preventive detention in cases where national security, public order, or other critical interests are at stake, albeit with strict procedural safeguards.

The Third Amendment in 1975 introduced significant changes to Article 10, reflecting the political climate and security concerns of the time. The amendment extended the maximum initial period of preventive detention from one month to three months before a review by the appropriate Review Board is required. This change likely aimed to give the government more leeway in dealing with perceived threats to national security. Additionally, the amendment extended the time frame within which the grounds for detention must be communicated to the detainee from one week to fifteen days, potentially reflecting the government's need for more time to manage complex security situations. Furthermore, the amendment broadened the exceptions to the maximum detention period, including those involved in anti-national activities or associated with organizations engaged in such activities, thus expanding the scope of individuals who could be detained for longer periods without the usual safeguards.

These changes underscore the tension between individual rights and national security, a common theme in constitutional law, especially in countries facing internal and external threats. The amendments to Article 10 reflect an attempt to balance these concerns, allowing the state to act decisively in the face of threats while still providing a framework for legal oversight and protection of individual rights.
