Article 74 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 establishes a crucial financial control mechanism within the legislative process. It mandates that any proposed legislation involving financial measures, such as a Money Bill or any bill that would result in expenditure from the Federal Consolidated Fund, withdrawal from the Public Account of the Federation, or affect the coinage, currency, or the State Bank of Pakistan, cannot be introduced in Parliament without the consent of the Federal Government. This provision ensures that the executive branch, represented by the Federal Government, retains significant control over financial legislation, thereby maintaining a balance of power between the legislative and executive branches.

The article's requirement for Federal Government consent is significant because it prevents the Parliament from unilaterally making financial decisions that could impact the country's economy or financial stability. By involving the Federal Government, which is responsible for the overall economic management of the country, this article ensures that financial legislation is aligned with national fiscal policies and priorities. This control is vital for maintaining economic discipline and preventing potentially reckless financial legislation that could lead to fiscal imbalances or undermine the country's monetary policy.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 74 has remained unchanged, indicating a consistent approach to financial governance in Pakistan's constitutional framework. The lack of amendments suggests that the original provision has been effective in achieving its intended purpose of safeguarding the nation's financial integrity while ensuring that the executive branch has a pivotal role in financial legislation. This stability reflects the importance of maintaining a robust system of checks and balances in the financial domain, which is essential for the country's economic health and governance.
