Article 151 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is concerned with the regulation of inter-provincial trade within the country. Its primary purpose is to ensure the free flow of trade, commerce, and intercourse across the provinces of Pakistan, which is crucial for maintaining economic unity and fostering national integration. This article establishes the principle that trade across the provinces should be unrestricted, thus promoting economic activity and cooperation among the provinces. However, it also recognizes the need for certain limitations in the public interest, granting Parliament the authority to impose restrictions if necessary.

The article delineates the powers of both the federal and provincial governments in relation to trade. While it primarily ensures that provincial governments cannot enact laws or take actions that would restrict the movement of goods across provincial borders, it also prevents them from imposing discriminatory taxes on goods based on their origin. This is intended to prevent economic protectionism within the country and to ensure a level playing field for goods produced in different regions of Pakistan. However, it does allow for exceptions where provincial assemblies can impose reasonable restrictions for specific purposes, such as public health or preventing shortages, provided these are made with the President's consent.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 151 has not been amended, indicating that its provisions have been deemed adequate to address the balance between free trade and necessary regulation. This stability suggests that the article effectively supports the constitutional framework's goal of economic cohesion and national unity, while also allowing for flexibility in addressing regional concerns.
