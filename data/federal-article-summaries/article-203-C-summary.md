Article 203C of the Constitution of Pakistan pertains to the constitution of the Federal Shariat Court, a judicial body that plays a crucial role in ensuring that laws in Pakistan conform to Islamic principles. This article outlines the appointment process and terms of service for the judges of the Federal Shariat Court, including the Chief Justice. It specifies that the President of Pakistan is responsible for appointing judges, who must have significant experience in Islamic law. The article also ensures that judges of the Federal Shariat Court receive remuneration and privileges comparable to those of judges in the Supreme Court and High Courts, thereby underscoring the importance of their role in the judicial system.

The article was first introduced through the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, which established the framework for appointing judges to the Federal Shariat Court. It included provisions for the appointment of Alim Judges, who are experts in Islamic law, and set terms for their service. The amendment also provided protections for the removal of judges, aligning it with the process for removing judges from the Supreme Court, thus ensuring judicial independence and integrity.

In 2024, the article was updated by the Twenty-sixth Amendment, although the specific changes made by this amendment are not detailed in the diff provided. The update signifies ongoing adjustments to the judicial framework to potentially address evolving legal and societal needs. These amendments reflect the dynamic nature of constitutional law in Pakistan, aiming to balance the integration of Islamic principles with the broader legal system, thereby impacting how laws are interpreted and applied in the country.
