Article 206 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the resignation of judges from the higher judiciary. Initially, it allowed a judge of the Supreme Court or a High Court to resign by submitting a written notice to the President. This provision is significant as it outlines the formal procedure for judges to voluntarily leave their positions, ensuring a clear and orderly process within the judiciary, which is a cornerstone of maintaining judicial independence and integrity.

The article has undergone two amendments, reflecting changes in the judicial structure and the conditions of service for judges. The Fifth Amendment in 1976 introduced a provision that a High Court judge who declines an appointment to the Supreme Court would be considered retired and entitled to a pension based on their service. This change aimed to encourage judges to accept promotions to the Supreme Court, thereby ensuring that the highest court benefits from experienced and capable judges, while also providing financial security to those who choose not to move up.

The Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025 further expanded the scope by including judges of a newly established Federal Constitutional Court. It specified that judges of the High Court or Supreme Court who refuse appointments to this new court would also be deemed retired, with pension entitlements. This amendment reflects an evolution in Pakistan's judicial system, possibly in response to a need for specialized constitutional adjudication, and underscores the importance of flexibility and incentives in judicial appointments to maintain an effective and dynamic judiciary.
