Article 216 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 is concerned with ensuring the impartiality and independence of the office of the Commissioner and its members by prohibiting them from holding any other office of profit in the service of Pakistan. This means that while serving as a Commissioner or a member, they cannot engage in any other employment that provides financial gain. Additionally, after their term ends, they are barred from holding any such office for two years. This provision is crucial as it aims to prevent conflicts of interest and maintain the integrity of the office, ensuring that those who serve are not influenced by other positions or financial incentives.

The article has undergone several amendments that have expanded and refined its scope. Initially, in 1973, it applied solely to the Commissioner. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 removed the provision that allowed a former Commissioner to be reappointed within two years with the concurrence of both Houses, thereby tightening restrictions on post-office employment. The Twentieth Amendment in 2012 extended these restrictions to include members of the office, not just the Commissioner, reflecting a broader approach to maintaining impartiality across the board. Finally, the Twenty-second Amendment in 2016 removed the clause that allowed former judges to resume their judicial duties immediately after serving as Commissioner, further emphasizing the separation of roles and preventing any potential bias or conflict of interest.

These amendments reflect a continuous effort to strengthen the independence of the office by ensuring that those who serve are not swayed by other positions or financial considerations, thereby upholding the integrity of the constitutional framework in Pakistan.
