Article 193 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the process and criteria for the appointment of judges to the High Courts. Initially established in the 1973 Constitution, it required the President to appoint judges after consulting with the Chief Justice of Pakistan, the relevant provincial governor, and the Chief Justice of the High Court, except when appointing the Chief Justice of a High Court. The article also set qualifications for judges, including citizenship, a minimum age of forty years, and specific professional experience, such as being an advocate or holding a judicial office for a certain period.

Over time, Article 193 has undergone several amendments that have refined the appointment process and qualifications for High Court judges. The First Amendment in 1974 added an explanation to clarify how to compute the period of experience required for advocates and judicial officers. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 significantly changed the appointment process by linking it to Article 175A, which introduced a more structured and transparent mechanism involving a judicial commission and a parliamentary committee. This amendment also raised the minimum age for appointment from forty to forty-five years. However, the Twenty-sixth Amendment in 2024 reverted the minimum age back to forty years and simplified the qualifications by removing the option of being a member of a civil service as a qualification criterion. The Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025 made minor textual adjustments without altering the substantive content of the article.

These amendments reflect ongoing efforts to balance the independence of the judiciary with accountability and transparency in judicial appointments. The changes have practical implications for the legal profession and the judiciary, as they affect who can qualify for these prestigious positions and how they are selected, thereby influencing the overall functioning and integrity of the judicial system in Pakistan.
