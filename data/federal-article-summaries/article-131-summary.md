Article 131 of the Constitution of Pakistan originally outlined the process for electing the Chief Minister of a province. It established that the Provincial Assembly must convene shortly after a general election, and after electing the Speaker and Deputy Speaker, the Assembly would elect one of its members as the Chief Minister. This election required a majority vote from the Assembly's total membership. If no candidate achieved a majority in the first round, a second round was held between the top two candidates. The process ensured that the Chief Minister had the confidence of the majority of the Assembly, which is crucial for maintaining a stable provincial government.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 significantly altered Article 131. The amendment removed the detailed election procedure for the Chief Minister and instead introduced a new requirement for the Chief Minister to keep the Governor informed about provincial administration and legislative proposals. This change shifted the focus from the election process to the relationship between the Chief Minister and the Governor, emphasizing transparency and communication in provincial governance. The amendment reflects a broader trend in the Eighteenth Amendment, which aimed to enhance provincial autonomy and improve governance by ensuring that provincial leaders are accountable and communicative with the Governor, who represents the federal government at the provincial level.
