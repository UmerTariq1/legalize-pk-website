Article 191 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, originally empowered the Supreme Court to establish its own rules for practice and procedure, provided these were in accordance with the Constitution and existing laws. This article is significant as it grants the judiciary a degree of autonomy in managing its internal processes, which is crucial for maintaining the independence of the judiciary. By allowing the Supreme Court to set its own procedural rules, the article ensures that the court can function efficiently and adapt to changing legal needs without undue interference from other branches of government.

The Twenty-seventh Amendment, enacted in 2025, brought a notable change to Article 191. It expanded the scope of the article by including the Federal Constitutional Court alongside the Supreme Court, allowing both courts to make rules regarding their respective practices and procedures. Additionally, the amendment specified that these rules must be subject not only to the Constitution and law but also to any Act of the Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament). This change reflects a shift towards a more structured oversight by the legislative branch, potentially aiming to ensure that the judiciary's procedural rules align with broader legislative objectives. The introduction of the Federal Constitutional Court into this framework suggests an evolving judicial structure, possibly in response to the need for specialized constitutional adjudication. This amendment could have practical effects by potentially increasing legislative influence over judicial procedures, while also recognizing the growing complexity of constitutional matters in Pakistan.
