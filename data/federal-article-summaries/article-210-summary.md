Article 210 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the powers of a Council to enforce the attendance of persons and the production of documents, similar to the powers held by the Supreme Court. This article is significant because it grants the Council judicial-like authority to compel individuals and documents necessary for inquiries, ensuring that the Council can effectively perform its investigative functions. The inclusion of this power underscores the importance of accountability and transparency in governance, as it enables thorough investigations into matters of public interest.

Originally, the article provided the Council with powers equivalent to those of the Supreme Court, allowing it to issue enforceable directions or orders for securing attendance and document production. This was crucial for maintaining the rule of law and ensuring that inquiries could be conducted with the same authority as the highest court in the land. The provisions of Article 204, which relate to contempt of court, were also applicable to the Council, further aligning its powers with those of the judiciary.

The Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, under the leadership of Asif Ali Zardari, expanded the scope of Article 210 by including the Federal Constitutional Court alongside the Supreme Court as a reference for the Council's powers. This amendment reflects a shift in the constitutional framework, possibly indicating a broader recognition of judicial bodies within Pakistan's legal system. By equating the Council's powers with those of both the Federal Constitutional Court and the Supreme Court, the amendment may have been aimed at reinforcing the Council's authority and ensuring its decisions are respected across different judicial platforms. This change could have been politically motivated to strengthen the Council's role in governance and oversight, ensuring it remains a robust mechanism for accountability.
