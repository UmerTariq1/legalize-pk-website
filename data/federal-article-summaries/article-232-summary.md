Article 232 of the Constitution of Pakistan deals with the proclamation of emergency in the country. It grants the President the authority to declare a state of emergency if the security of Pakistan is threatened by war, external aggression, or internal disturbances that a provincial government cannot control. This article is crucial as it outlines the conditions under which the federal government can assume greater control over provincial matters, thereby centralizing authority in times of national crisis. It also specifies the legislative powers of the Parliament during such emergencies, allowing it to make laws for provinces on matters usually outside its jurisdiction.

The article has undergone significant changes through constitutional amendments. Initially, as per the 1973 Constitution, the President could declare an emergency unilaterally. However, the Third Amendment in 1975 introduced a check by allowing a joint sitting of Parliament to disapprove the proclamation, thereby limiting the duration of an emergency unless approved by Parliament. This change was likely motivated by a desire to prevent the abuse of emergency powers and ensure democratic oversight.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 further refined the process by requiring a resolution from the provincial assembly for emergencies due to internal disturbances, thus enhancing provincial autonomy. Additionally, if the President declared an emergency on his own, it had to be approved by both Houses of Parliament within ten days. These changes reflect a shift towards a more balanced distribution of power between the federal and provincial governments and aim to prevent the central government from overriding provincial authority without due process.
