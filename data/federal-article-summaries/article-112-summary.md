Article 112 of the Constitution of Pakistan deals with the dissolution of a Provincial Assembly. It establishes the conditions under which a Provincial Assembly can be dissolved, either by the Governor on the advice of the Chief Minister or at the Governor's discretion with the President's approval. This article is significant as it outlines the balance of power between the provincial executive and the legislature, ensuring that the Chief Minister, who must maintain the confidence of the assembly, cannot unilaterally dissolve it if they are under a no-confidence motion. This mechanism is crucial for maintaining political stability and accountability within the provincial governments.

The article has undergone several amendments reflecting shifts in political power and governance philosophy in Pakistan. Initially, in 1973, it allowed the Governor to dissolve the assembly on the Chief Minister's advice unless a no-confidence motion was pending against the Chief Minister. The Eighth Amendment in 1985, during General Zia-ul-Haq's regime, expanded the Governor's powers, allowing dissolution with the President's approval if no member could command a majority or if the provincial government could not function constitutionally. This change was part of broader efforts to centralize power and control over provincial matters.

Subsequent amendments, such as the Thirteenth in 1997 and the Seventeenth in 2003, adjusted these provisions, with the latter requiring the Supreme Court's involvement in certain dissolution cases. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, a significant constitutional reform under President Asif Ali Zardari, streamlined the article by removing the Supreme Court's role and simplifying the conditions under which the Governor could dissolve the assembly. These changes reflect ongoing efforts to balance provincial autonomy with federal oversight, a recurring theme in Pakistan's constitutional evolution.
