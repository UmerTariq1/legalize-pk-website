Article 165 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the exemption of certain public properties from taxation. This article establishes a rule that the Federal Government's property or income is not subject to taxation by any Provincial Assembly. Similarly, a Provincial Government's property or income is exempt from taxation by the Federal Parliament or by another Province's Assembly. This provision is significant as it delineates the financial autonomy and jurisdictional boundaries between federal and provincial governments, ensuring that governmental entities are not financially burdened by each other's tax laws, which could otherwise complicate intergovernmental relations and operations.

However, the article also introduces a specific condition under which a Provincial Government can be taxed. If a provincial government conducts trade or business outside its own province, it can be taxed on the property used or income generated from that business by the Parliament or the Provincial Assembly where the business is conducted. This clause ensures that when a provincial government engages in commercial activities beyond its borders, it contributes to the local economy through taxes, similar to any other business entity operating in that region.

Additionally, the article clarifies that it does not prevent the imposition of fees for services rendered. This means that while the governments are exempt from certain taxes, they are still liable to pay fees for specific services they utilize, ensuring that service providers are compensated. This article is crucial in maintaining a balance between governmental financial independence and accountability, facilitating smooth governance and fiscal management across different levels of government in Pakistan. Since its inception in 1973, this article has remained unchanged, indicating its foundational role in the constitutional framework.
