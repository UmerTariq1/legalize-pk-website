Article 104 of the Constitution of Pakistan, originally established in 1973, addresses the procedure for appointing an acting Governor when the sitting Governor is unable to perform their duties. Initially, the article allowed the President of Pakistan to appoint any person to act as Governor in such circumstances. This provision was crucial as it ensured the continuity of governance at the provincial level, maintaining stability and preventing any administrative vacuum in the absence of the Governor.

The Eighteenth Amendment, enacted in 2010, significantly altered this article. It specified that the Speaker of the Provincial Assembly would act as the Governor when the Governor is absent or unable to fulfill their duties. If the Speaker is unavailable, the President can nominate another person to assume the role. This change was part of a broader effort to enhance provincial autonomy and reduce the central government's direct influence over provincial matters. By prioritizing the Speaker of the Provincial Assembly, the amendment aimed to strengthen the provincial legislative body's role and ensure that someone familiar with the province's legislative affairs would temporarily assume the Governor's responsibilities. This shift reflects a move towards a more decentralized governance structure, aligning with the democratic principles of empowering local institutions and representatives.
