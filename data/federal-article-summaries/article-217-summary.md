Article 217 of the Constitution of Pakistan, originally established in 1973, outlines the procedure for appointing an Acting Commissioner when the office of the Commissioner is vacant or when the Commissioner is unable to perform their duties. This provision is crucial as it ensures the continuity of the Commission's functions, which could be related to various administrative or judicial responsibilities, depending on the context in which the term "Commissioner" is applied within the constitutional framework. The article originally designated a Judge of the Supreme Court, nominated by the Chief Justice of Pakistan, to act as the Commissioner in such circumstances. This choice underscored the importance of maintaining a high level of judicial oversight and integrity in the functioning of the Commission.

In 2016, the Twenty-second Amendment brought a significant change to this article. The amendment shifted the responsibility from a Supreme Court Judge to the most senior member in age of the members of the Commission. This change reflects a move towards internal continuity within the Commission itself, rather than relying on external judicial figures. The practical effect of this amendment is that it potentially speeds up the process of appointing an Acting Commissioner by utilizing existing Commission members, thereby ensuring that the Commission's work is not disrupted. This change could also be seen as an effort to empower the Commission's internal structure and reduce dependency on the judiciary for administrative continuity.
