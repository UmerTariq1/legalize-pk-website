Article 69 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 is designed to protect the autonomy and internal proceedings of the Parliament from judicial interference. This article ensures that the courts cannot question the validity of parliamentary proceedings based on procedural irregularities. Essentially, it establishes a separation of powers by safeguarding the legislative process from being challenged or scrutinized by the judiciary. This is crucial in maintaining the independence of the legislative branch, allowing it to function without the threat of judicial intervention in its procedural matters.

The article also specifies that no officer or member of Parliament, who is empowered by the Constitution to regulate parliamentary procedures or maintain order, can be subjected to the jurisdiction of any court regarding the exercise of these powers. This provision further reinforces the autonomy of parliamentary operations, ensuring that those responsible for conducting parliamentary business can do so without fear of legal repercussions. By doing so, it upholds the principle that Parliament is the master of its own proceedings, a fundamental aspect of parliamentary sovereignty.

Since its inception in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 69 has remained unchanged. This consistency underscores the enduring importance of maintaining a clear boundary between the legislative and judicial branches of government. By preventing courts from intervening in parliamentary matters, this article helps preserve the balance of power within Pakistan's constitutional framework, ensuring that each branch of government can operate within its own domain effectively.
