Article 128 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, grants the Governor of a province the power to issue ordinances when the Provincial Assembly is not in session. This provision is significant as it allows for immediate legislative action in situations deemed urgent by the Governor, ensuring that governance can continue smoothly even when the Assembly is not convened. The ordinances issued under this article carry the same weight as laws passed by the Provincial Assembly, but they are temporary measures that must be presented to the Assembly for approval. If the Assembly does not approve the ordinance within a specified period, it automatically lapses.

The original text of Article 128, as established in 1973, allowed ordinances to remain in effect for three months unless disapproved by the Provincial Assembly. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 introduced a notable change by extending the potential lifespan of an ordinance. It allowed the Provincial Assembly to extend an ordinance for an additional 120 days, but only once. This amendment aimed to provide more flexibility in governance while ensuring that ordinances do not become a substitute for regular legislative processes. The change reflects a balance between the need for swift executive action and the democratic principle of legislative oversight, ensuring that the power to legislate remains primarily with the elected representatives in the Assembly.
