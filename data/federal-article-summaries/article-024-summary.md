Article 24 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is concerned with the protection of property rights. It establishes the fundamental principle that no person shall be deprived of their property except in accordance with the law. This article is significant as it safeguards individuals' property rights, ensuring that any deprivation of property must be legally justified and compensated. The protection of property rights is a cornerstone of personal liberty and economic stability, as it provides citizens with the assurance that their property cannot be arbitrarily taken by the state or other entities.

The article outlines specific conditions under which property can be compulsorily acquired by the state. It stipulates that such acquisition must be for a public purpose and must be authorized by law, which also provides for compensation. This compensation must either be predetermined or based on principles specified by the law. The article also lists exceptions where the state can acquire property without the usual constraints, such as for preventing danger to life or public health, managing property in the public interest, or acquiring property for public services like education, medical aid, and infrastructure development.

Importantly, Article 24 also states that the adequacy of compensation provided for such acquisitions cannot be challenged in court. This provision underscores the state's authority in determining compensation, potentially limiting judicial oversight in disputes over compensation adequacy. The article, as originally drafted in 1973, has not been amended, indicating a consistent approach to property rights and state authority in this domain since the inception of the Constitution. This stability reflects the importance of balancing individual property rights with the needs of the public and the state’s role in facilitating public welfare and development.
