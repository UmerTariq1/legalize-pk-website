Article 139 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, addresses the conduct of business by the Provincial Government. Its primary purpose is to outline how orders and instruments made in the name of the Governor are to be authenticated and how the business of the Provincial Government is to be conducted. This article is significant as it ensures a structured and consistent approach to governance at the provincial level, providing clarity on the delegation of functions and the execution of government orders. By stipulating that orders authenticated in a specified manner cannot be questioned on the grounds of not being executed by the Governor, it upholds the integrity and authority of provincial governance processes.

The article was originally part of the 1973 Constitution and remained unchanged until the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. This amendment introduced a minor but meaningful change by specifying that the delegation of functions could be to officers or authorities subordinate to the "Provincial Government," rather than just "subordinate to it." This clarification reinforced the hierarchical structure within the provincial administration. Additionally, a new clause was added, mandating that the Provincial Government must make rules for the allocation and transaction of its business. This addition aimed to enhance transparency and accountability by ensuring that there are clear guidelines governing how provincial business is conducted. The Eighteenth Amendment, a significant reform package, sought to decentralize power and strengthen provincial autonomy, and the changes to Article 139 were part of this broader effort to empower provincial governments and streamline their operations.
