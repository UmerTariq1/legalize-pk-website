Article 184 of the Constitution of Pakistan originally established the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction, meaning its authority to hear certain types of cases first, rather than on appeal. Specifically, it allowed the Supreme Court to exclusively handle disputes between the Federal Government and the Provincial Governments. This provision was significant as it positioned the Supreme Court as the ultimate arbiter in inter-governmental conflicts, ensuring a centralized judicial mechanism to resolve such disputes. Additionally, the article empowered the Supreme Court to issue declaratory judgments in these cases and to address questions of public importance related to the enforcement of fundamental rights, highlighting its role in safeguarding constitutional rights.

The article underwent significant changes with the Twenty-sixth and Twenty-seventh Amendments. The Twenty-sixth Amendment, enacted in 2024, introduced a limitation on the Supreme Court's power to act on its own initiative, known as suo motu jurisdiction, in matters of public importance related to fundamental rights. This change required that the Court's actions be based on applications filed, rather than on its own accord, potentially curbing the Court's proactive role in addressing public interest issues. This amendment likely reflected a political decision to balance judicial activism with procedural constraints, possibly in response to concerns about judicial overreach.

In 2025, the Twenty-seventh Amendment further altered Article 184 by omitting its content entirely. This removal effectively stripped the Supreme Court of its original jurisdiction as defined in the article, marking a profound shift in the judicial landscape. The omission could have been driven by political motivations to redefine the balance of power between the judiciary and other branches of government, or to streamline judicial processes by reallocating jurisdictional responsibilities. The practical effect of these amendments would have been a significant reduction in the Supreme Court's direct involvement in inter-governmental disputes and fundamental rights issues, potentially impacting how such matters are addressed within Pakistan's constitutional framework.
