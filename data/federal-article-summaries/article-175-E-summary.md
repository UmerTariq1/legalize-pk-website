Article 175-E, introduced through the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, establishes the Federal Constitutional Court in Pakistan, granting it exclusive original jurisdiction over disputes between the Federal Government and Provincial Governments. This means that any legal conflicts involving these governmental entities must be initially addressed by this court, rather than any other judicial body. The creation of this court is significant as it centralizes constitutional adjudication, aiming to streamline and possibly expedite the resolution of intergovernmental disputes, which can be complex and politically sensitive.

The article also empowers the Federal Constitutional Court to issue declaratory judgments, which are formal statements that clarify the legal position of the parties involved without necessarily ordering any specific action. Moreover, the court is given the authority to address questions of public importance related to the enforcement of fundamental rights, provided that an application is filed by an individual. This provision underscores the court's role in safeguarding constitutional rights and ensuring that significant legal questions are addressed at the highest level.

Additionally, the amendment mandates the transfer of relevant pending petitions and appeals from the Supreme Court to the newly established Federal Constitutional Court. This transfer reflects a restructuring of the judicial process, concentrating certain constitutional matters within a specialized court. The Federal Constitutional Court is also granted the power to review cases involving substantial constitutional questions, allowing it to intervene in ongoing cases if deemed necessary. This amendment, spearheaded by Asif Ali Zardari, represents a significant shift in Pakistan's judicial landscape, potentially affecting how constitutional issues are managed and resolved in the country.
