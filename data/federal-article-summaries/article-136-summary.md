Article 136 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, establishes the procedure for a vote of no-confidence against a Chief Minister in a Provincial Assembly. This article is crucial as it provides a mechanism for the Provincial Assembly to express its lack of confidence in the Chief Minister, thereby ensuring accountability and responsiveness of the provincial executive to the elected representatives. The article outlines the conditions under which such a resolution can be moved, including the requirement that a successor's name must be proposed simultaneously, and it sets specific timeframes for when the resolution can be voted upon. This ensures that the process is orderly and that there is a clear plan for succession, preventing any power vacuum.

The article also includes a provision that, for a period of ten years from the commencement of the Constitution or until the second general election, the vote of a member elected as a candidate or nominee of a political party in support of a no-confidence resolution will be disregarded if the majority of that party's members oppose the resolution. This clause was likely included to maintain party discipline and prevent political instability during the early years of the Constitution's implementation. Additionally, if a no-confidence resolution fails, the article stipulates that another such resolution cannot be moved for six months, which prevents the misuse of the no-confidence mechanism and ensures stability in the provincial government.

Since its inception in 1973, Article 136 has not been amended, indicating that its original provisions have been deemed effective in balancing the need for governmental stability with the accountability of the Chief Minister to the Provincial Assembly. This article plays a significant role in the political dynamics of Pakistan's provinces by providing a structured process for leadership change while safeguarding against frequent and potentially destabilizing challenges to the Chief Minister's authority.
