Article 46 of the Constitution of Pakistan, established in 1973, mandates that the Prime Minister must keep the President informed about matters concerning internal and foreign policy, as well as any legislative proposals that the Federal Government plans to present to Parliament. This article is significant as it ensures a flow of information between the executive branches of government, promoting transparency and coordination. By keeping the President informed, the article helps maintain a balance of power and allows the President to fulfill their constitutional role effectively, even though the President's role is largely ceremonial in Pakistan's parliamentary system.

The article underwent a minor amendment with the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. The change was subtle yet symbolically important: it replaced the term "Parliament" with "Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament)." This amendment did not alter the substance of the article but rather aligned the terminology with the constitutional language, reflecting a more culturally and linguistically appropriate term for the legislative body. The Eighteenth Amendment was part of a broader effort to decentralize power and restore parliamentary sovereignty, and while this specific change in Article 46 was minor, it was part of a larger context of constitutional reforms aimed at strengthening democratic institutions in Pakistan.
