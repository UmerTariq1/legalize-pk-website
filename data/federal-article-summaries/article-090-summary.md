Article 90 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the structure and functioning of the Federal Government. Initially established in 1973, this article defines the executive authority of the Federation, which is exercised in the name of the President by the Federal Government. This government consists of the Prime Minister and the Federal Ministers, with the Prime Minister serving as the chief executive. The article emphasizes the collective responsibility of the Prime Minister and the Federal Ministers to the National Assembly, ensuring a system of accountability and parliamentary oversight.

The article has undergone significant changes through constitutional amendments. The Eighth Amendment in 1985, during General Zia-ul-Haq's regime, introduced provisions that clarified the distribution of functions between the President and other authorities. It ensured that the President's role did not encroach upon the functions conferred by existing laws on provincial governments or other authorities, and it allowed Parliament to assign functions to authorities other than the President. This amendment was part of broader changes that increased the President's powers, reflecting the political dynamics of Zia's military rule.

The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010, under President Asif Ali Zardari, reversed some of the changes made by the Eighth Amendment. It removed the clauses added in 1985, thereby reducing the President's influence over the executive functions and reinforcing the parliamentary system by emphasizing the Prime Minister's role. This amendment was part of a larger effort to restore the balance of power between the President and the Parliament, reflecting a shift towards strengthening democratic institutions and reducing the remnants of military influence in governance.
