Article 45 of the Constitution of Pakistan, established in 1973, grants the President the authority to pardon, reprieve, or commute sentences handed down by courts, tribunals, or other authorities. This power is a crucial aspect of the executive branch's role in the justice system, allowing for a check on judicial decisions and providing a mechanism for mercy and clemency. The ability to pardon or commute sentences can be especially significant in cases where there may be doubts about the fairness of a trial, where new evidence has emerged, or where there are humanitarian considerations. This power reflects the principle that justice should be tempered with mercy and that the executive branch has a role in ensuring that the legal system remains just and humane.

Since its inception, Article 45 has not been amended, indicating a consistent recognition of the importance of this presidential power in Pakistan's constitutional framework. The unaltered nature of this article suggests a broad consensus on the necessity of maintaining this executive prerogative as a means of addressing potential miscarriages of justice and providing relief in exceptional circumstances. The President's clemency powers serve as a vital tool for maintaining balance within the legal system, ensuring that the rigid application of law does not lead to undue hardship or injustice.
