Article 101 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the appointment and qualifications of the Governor for each province. Initially established in the 1973 Constitution, it mandates that the President appoints a Governor for each province. The Governor must be at least 35 years old and qualified to be elected as a member of the National Assembly. The Governor serves at the pleasure of the President and can resign by addressing a letter to the President. This article is significant as it establishes the role of the Governor as the ceremonial head of the province, representing the federal government's interests at the provincial level.

Over the years, Article 101 has undergone several amendments, reflecting shifts in political power and governance philosophy. The First Amendment in 1974 added that the Governor would receive a salary, allowances, and privileges as determined by the President. The Fifth Amendment in 1976 introduced a residency requirement, stipulating that a Governor should not be a permanent resident of the province they govern, although this was subject to suspension during emergencies. The Seventh Amendment in 1977 slightly modified the emergency provisions. The Eighth Amendment in 1985, under General Zia-ul-Haq, required the President to consult with the Prime Minister when appointing a Governor, adding a layer of political collaboration.

The Thirteenth Amendment in 1997 further strengthened the Prime Minister's role by changing the requirement from consultation to appointment on the Prime Minister's advice, thus reducing the President's discretion. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 added that the Governor must be a registered voter and resident of the province they are appointed to, ensuring a closer connection to the province's populace. These changes reflect a broader trend towards increasing provincial autonomy and ensuring that Governors are more representative of the provinces they serve, aligning with democratic principles and local governance needs.
