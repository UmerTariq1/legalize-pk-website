Article 54 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 outlines the procedures for summoning and proroguing Parliament, which includes both the National Assembly and the Senate. This article is crucial as it establishes the framework for how and when Parliament can be convened, ensuring that legislative processes are conducted in an orderly manner. It grants the President the authority to summon either House or both Houses of Parliament to meet at a time and place of his choosing and also allows him to prorogue, or discontinue, a session without dissolving it. Additionally, the article mandates that the National Assembly must meet for at least two sessions each year, with no more than 120 days between sessions, ensuring regular legislative activity.

The evolution of Article 54 through amendments reflects changes in the political landscape and governance needs of Pakistan. The Fourth Amendment in 1975 introduced an explanation to clarify that "working days" include any day with a joint sitting and short adjournments, ensuring clarity in legislative scheduling. This amendment was likely aimed at preventing any ambiguity that could disrupt the legislative calendar. The Tenth Amendment in 1987 significantly altered the requirement for the National Assembly's annual meetings, reducing the minimum number of working days from 130 to 30. This change, occurring during Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq's regime, could be seen as an effort to streamline legislative processes or reduce the burden on the Assembly, reflecting the political priorities of that period.

These amendments to Article 54 have practical implications for how Parliament operates, affecting the frequency and duration of legislative sessions. By adjusting the minimum number of working days, the amendments have influenced how often Parliament convenes, which can impact the legislative output and the government's ability to pass laws efficiently. The changes also reflect broader political strategies and shifts in governance, illustrating how constitutional provisions can be adapted to meet the evolving needs of a country's political system.
