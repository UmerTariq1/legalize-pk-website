Article 243 of the Constitution of Pakistan outlines the command and control of the Armed Forces, a crucial aspect of national governance. Initially established in the 1973 Constitution, it vested the Federal Government with control over the Armed Forces, while the President was empowered to raise and maintain military forces, grant commissions, and appoint the chiefs of the Army, Navy, and Air Force. This article is significant as it delineates the civilian oversight of the military, a fundamental principle in maintaining the balance of power within a democratic state.

Over the years, Article 243 has undergone several amendments, reflecting shifts in political dynamics and governance structures. The Thirteenth Amendment in 1997 did not alter the text but marked a period of political transition. The Seventeenth Amendment in 2003, under President Pervez Musharraf, also left the article unchanged, but it was a time when the military's role in politics was pronounced. The Eighteenth Amendment in 2010 introduced a significant change by specifying that the President would appoint military chiefs on the advice of the Prime Minister, reinforcing the role of the civilian government in military appointments.

The most substantial changes came with the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 2025, which restructured military command roles. It abolished the office of the Chairman, Joint Chiefs of Staff Committee, and introduced the position of Chief of the Defense Forces, concurrently held by the Chief of the Army Staff. This amendment also introduced the concept of lifetime ranks for top military officers like Field Marshal, ensuring their continued influence and privileges. These changes reflect an evolving approach to military leadership and its integration with civilian oversight, aiming to streamline command structures while maintaining the ceremonial and strategic roles of senior military figures.
