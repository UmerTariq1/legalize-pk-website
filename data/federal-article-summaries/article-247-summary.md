Article 247 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973 originally dealt with the administration of Tribal Areas, specifically the Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA) and Provincially Administered Tribal Areas (PATA). This article outlined the unique governance structure for these regions, granting significant powers to the President and provincial governors. The President had the authority to direct the application of laws and make regulations for peace and good governance in these areas. The article also restricted the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and High Courts over these regions unless Parliament decided otherwise. This framework was designed to accommodate the distinct tribal customs and governance systems, reflecting the historical and socio-political complexities of these areas.

The Twenty-fifth Amendment, enacted on May 31, 2018, marked a significant shift by omitting Article 247 entirely. This amendment was part of a broader effort to integrate FATA into the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province, thereby abolishing the separate administrative status that these tribal areas held. The omission of Article 247 effectively extended the jurisdiction of Pakistani laws and the judiciary to these regions, aligning them with the rest of the country. This change aimed to bring political and legal uniformity, enhance development, and address longstanding grievances of the tribal populations by providing them with equal rights and representation within the provincial framework. The amendment was a major political move, reflecting a consensus to mainstream the tribal areas into the national fold, thereby ending decades of semi-autonomous governance.
