Article 14 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is a fundamental rights provision that emphasizes the inviolability of human dignity and the privacy of the home. It establishes two key principles: first, that the dignity of every individual is to be respected and protected, and second, that the privacy of one's home is safeguarded, subject to the law. Additionally, it explicitly prohibits the use of torture for the purpose of extracting evidence. This article is crucial as it underscores the state's commitment to upholding human rights and personal freedoms, reflecting the values of respect and protection for individuals within society.

Since its inception in the original 1973 Constitution, Article 14 has not been amended. This consistency highlights the enduring importance of the principles it enshrines. The protection of human dignity and the prohibition of torture are fundamental to ensuring that citizens are treated with respect and humanity, which are essential components of a just and fair society. By safeguarding the privacy of the home, the article also reinforces the idea that individuals have a right to a private life free from unwarranted intrusion, which is a cornerstone of personal liberty. The unaltered nature of this article suggests a broad consensus on these values across different political eras in Pakistan.
