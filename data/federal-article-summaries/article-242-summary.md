Article 242 of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973, is concerned with the establishment and functioning of Public Service Commissions at both the federal and provincial levels. The purpose of these commissions is to ensure a fair and merit-based recruitment process for civil services, which is crucial for maintaining an efficient and impartial bureaucracy. This article empowers the Parliament and Provincial Assemblies to create laws for setting up these commissions, which are tasked with performing functions as defined by the law. The establishment of Public Service Commissions is significant as it helps in maintaining transparency and accountability in the recruitment process, thereby upholding the principles of good governance.

The article underwent a significant change with the Eighteenth Amendment in 2010. Prior to this amendment, the article simply allowed for the creation of Public Service Commissions and left their functions to be defined by law. The amendment introduced a new clause (1B), specifying that the Chairman of a Provincial Public Service Commission would be appointed by the Governor on the advice of the Chief Minister. This change was part of a broader effort to decentralize power and give more autonomy to provincial governments, reflecting a shift towards strengthening provincial governance structures. By involving the Chief Minister in the appointment process, the amendment aimed to ensure that provincial interests are better represented and that the commissions are more responsive to the specific needs of each province. This change has practical implications for the balance of power between federal and provincial authorities and underscores the importance of provincial autonomy in Pakistan's federal structure.
