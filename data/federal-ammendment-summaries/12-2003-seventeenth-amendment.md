After General Pervez Musharraf took power in a 1999 coup, he operated for years under "Emergency" rule and a "Legal Framework Order" (LFO). However, to gain international and domestic legitimacy, he needed to transition to a semi-civilian system. The 2002 elections resulted in a hung parliament where Musharraf's supporters needed the help of the religious political alliance (MMA) to pass constitutional changes. The 17th Amendment was the result of this "grand bargain." Musharraf wanted his military decrees validated and his powers as President solidified, while the opposition pushed for some judicial oversight and a deadline for him to take off his military uniform.

The 17th Amendment was the second time in Pakistan's history that the country shifted from a Parliamentary system back to a Semi-Presidential one. The "then vs. now" shift was a reversal of the 13th Amendment. Once again, the Prime Minister became secondary to the President. The President was no longer just a figurehead; he was the man who could shut down Parliament if he felt the "government could not be carried on."

For the average citizen, this meant that political power was concentrated back into the hands of a single individual who also happened to be the head of the military. While the requirement to involve the Supreme Court in the dissolution of Parliament was marketed as a "safety net," it effectively pulled the judiciary into the heat of political battles. It created a system where the "referee" (the Court) was constantly asked to decide if the "owner" (the President) was right to fire the "team" (the Parliament), leading to years of political and legal instability.
